# 7. Canopy Architecture and Pruning

> **Evidence callout.** Pruning *principles* (thinning vs heading, light penetration, wound
> sanitation) are **strong** — peer-reviewed and extension [C078](#claim-c078), [A096](#claim-a096), [D042](#claim-d042). Lisbon-specific
> and container-specific pruning is **weak-to-moderate** (consumer/nursery) [C056](#claim-c056), [A122](#claim-a122) but
> consistent. One apparent contradiction — "open the canopy for light" vs "don't thin, keep it
> dense for sunburn protection" — is **reconciled** below; it is the section's organizing idea.
> This section is the **home** for canopy pruning; **root pruning lives in §5** (cross-ref for
> the root:canopy balance), structural support is §8, sunburn protection is §15, disease
> sanitation detail is §13.

Lisbon is the most vigorous lemon grown in California (§1), so it **requires active canopy
management** for height, light, and fruit access [A078](#claim-a078). But pruning is a double-edged tool:
it is **dwarfing for the tree as a whole yet locally invigorating at each cut** [C079](#claim-c079). On a
vigorous, thorny scion that means heavy cutting backfires — it provokes a thicket of thorny
water-sprouts and *less* fruit [C079](#claim-c079), [A081](#claim-a081). **Light, regular, selective shaping is the rule;
hard heading is the trap.**

---

## 7.1 The Two Cut Types (strong, peer-reviewed)

| Cut | What it does | Use it to | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Thinning** | Removes a whole shoot back to its origin | **Open the canopy without dense regrowth** | C078 | Strong |
| **Heading** | Shortens a shoot | Provokes **vigorous, bushy, thorny regrowth** — use sparingly | C078, C079 | Strong |

> **Core rule:** favor **thinning over heading**; over-heading produces a water-sprout thicket
> and reduces the next crop [C079](#claim-c079), [A081](#claim-a081). **Light regular pruning beats infrequent heavy
> pruning** [C080](#claim-c080), [A121](#claim-a121); citrus does not need the annual heavy pruning deciduous fruit does
> [C329](#claim-c329). Many small cuts seal faster and safer than a few large ones [D043](#claim-d043).

---

## 7.2 The Central Reconciliation: Open *Interior*, Dense *Exterior* ⚠️

> ⚠️ **Apparent conflict.** "The biggest long-term benefit of pruning is **light penetration**
> into the canopy interior, increasing flowering/interior fruiting" [A096](#claim-a096) — but also "**do NOT
> thin the canopy**; a dense canopy shades and protects trunk/branches from sunburn" [A123,
> B125].
>
> **Reconciliation (claim-table-aligned):** these describe *different parts of the tree*.
> **Open the center** — remove crossing, inward, dead, and water-sprout growth to let light and
> air into the interior [C058](#claim-c058), [C103](#claim-c103), [C099](#claim-c099) — **while keeping the outer canopy dense** so foliage
> shades the trunk and scaffolds from sunburn [A123](#claim-a123), [C058](#claim-c058), [B125](#claim-b125). The target form is
> **open-center/vase: airy inside, leafy outside** [C099](#claim-c099). Lisbon's habit makes this especially
> apt, because its fruit is borne **inside** the canopy (§1), so interior light directly buys
> interior fruit [A096](#claim-a096), [E028](#claim-e028).

This matters acutely on a west wall: over-thinning exposes bark to sudden summer sun and
**bark sunburn strikes wood newly exposed by pruning** [A080](#claim-a080), [B123](#claim-b123) (protection: §15).

## 7.3 What to Remove (general + Lisbon)

| Target | Action | Why | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rootstock suckers** (below graft) | **Pull downward to tear off cleanly** at the base; dig to sever root suckers | They are the (bitter trifoliate/sour-orange) rootstock, bear inedible fruit, and out-vigor the scion | B130, B131, B132 | Strong |
| **Water-sprouts** (above graft) | Manage **selectively** — keep some as future fruiting wood, remove the rest to open the canopy | Real scion wood; vigorous and thorniest | B133, C057 | Strong / Weak |
| Crossing / inward / dead | Thin out | Airflow, light, disease | C058, C103 | Weak / Moderate |
| Low skirt branches | Remove branches near the soil/pot rim (~within 30 in in-ground) | Cuts fruit decay (brown-rot splash); improves airflow | A023 | Strong |
| Thorns | **Do not snip individual thorns** — remove whole thorny/crossing branches in dormancy | A thorn cut is an infection entry point | C056 | Weak |

**Sucker-suppression aid:** a white cardboard/wrap collar around the **lower 10–12 in** of
trunk discourages low sucker growth; don't paint sucker wounds [B134](#claim-b134).

## 7.4 Timing (reconciled across sources)

| Window | Verdict | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Just after main harvest** | **Best** — flower buds form on last summer's growth, so this preserves fruiting wood and new flushes | A124, C003 | Strong / Weak |
| Late winter–early spring (after frost risk passes) | Good — before new growth, or right after spring bloom reveals where fruit will set | C100, C081 | Moderate / Strong |
| **Late summer / fall** | **Avoid** — pushes tender growth vulnerable to cold and removes next season's buds | C101 | Moderate |
| Mid-summer heat | Avoid — fresh cuts/exposed bark + heat = sunburn | C081 | Strong |

> **Synthesis:** prune in the **post-harvest → late-winter/early-spring** window, **never in
> late summer/fall**, and avoid mid-summer heat [A124](#claim-a124), [C081](#claim-c081), [C101](#claim-c101). Pair the structural prune
> with the 2–3-yr root-prune cycle (§5) to keep **root:canopy balance** [C023](#claim-c023), [D068](#claim-d068).

## 7.5 Life-Stage Program (container citrus)

| Stage | Program | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Years 1–3 (establishment)** | Build framework: select well-spaced scaffolds; remove suckers/inward/crossing; **light ~10–15%/yr removal** | A122, C001 | Strong / Weak |
| **Mature** | **Light maintenance only** — shape, remove deadwood/suckers, contain height, keep canopy dense | C002, C305 | Weak |
| Both | Prune **gently, mainly for shape/size**, not heavily | A121 | Strong |

## 7.6 Compact and Specialty Forms (container, moderate/weak)

| Form | Method | Note | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Open-center / vase | Thin interior, keep outer density | Light + air inside, sunburn protection outside | C099 | Moderate |
| Patio bush | **Tip-prune terminal buds** to force outward bushy growth (vs leggy/upward) | Keeps a tidy free-standing shape | C098 | Moderate |
| Espalier | Prune **~2×/yr** (summer + winter) | Citrus espaliers well and is forgiving | C096 | Moderate |
| **Bonsai/miniature** | Ramify: cut new shoots back to ~2 leaves after 4–6 nodes; clip-and-grow; defoliation (advanced) | **Trades fruit for miniaturization** — use sparingly on a fruiting tree | D059, D060, D062 | Weak |

> ⚠️ Heavy pinching/defoliation/ramification miniaturizes leaf size and tree [D060](#claim-d060) but
> **sacrifices crop** [D062](#claim-d062) — not recommended if fruit is the goal. **Pruning is not a
> substitute for a dwarfing rootstock** (§2): size control comes from Flying Dragon + root
> pruning; canopy pruning shapes and refines.

## 7.7 Tools and Wound Sanitation (strong)

| Practice | Specification | Claim | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sharp, clean tools | Clean cuts seal up to **~40% faster** than ragged ones; dull tools crush tissue → slow-healing entry points | D042 | Strong |
| Cut size | Favor **many small cuts over few large** (small wounds callus faster) | D043 | Strong |
| Disinfect between plants/diseased cuts | **70% isopropyl alcohol or 10% bleach** — prevents viroid (exocortis) spread | D044 | Strong |
| Don't disinfect the wound | Disinfectant on the cut injures tissue/slows callus; wound dressings only worth it on large cuts | D045 | Strong |

---

> ### 🪴 Worked example — 19″×14″ west-facing Brentwood / West LA balcony
> - **Form:** keep an **open-center, airy interior with a dense leafy exterior** [C099](#claim-c099), [A123](#claim-a123).
>   The west sun makes the "keep outer density" half non-negotiable — over-thinning here invites
>   bark sunburn [A080](#claim-a080), [B123](#claim-b123) (§15).
> - **Thin, don't head.** Remove inward/crossing growth and most water-sprouts (keep a few for
>   fruiting wood) by **thinning cuts** [C078](#claim-c078), [B133](#claim-b133); heading just breeds thorny sprouts [C079](#claim-c079).
> - **Pull rootstock suckers off** below the graft — Flying Dragon is bitter trifoliate and will
>   out-vigor the scion if left [B130](#claim-b130), [B131](#claim-b131); a white collar on the lower 10–12″ helps [B134](#claim-b134).
> - **Timing:** prune **right after the winter main-crop harvest** [A124](#claim-a124) (and tidy in late
>   winter), paired with the late-winter root-prune (§5) for root:canopy balance [C023](#claim-c023). Never
>   prune late summer/fall or in a heat wave [C101](#claim-c101), [C081](#claim-c081).
> - **Manage thorns by removing whole branches in dormancy**, never by snipping individual
>   thorns [C056](#claim-c056); thorniest wood is the young sprouts you're removing anyway [C057](#claim-c057).
> - **Tools:** sharp bypass pruners, **wipe with 70% isopropyl** between sessions [D044](#claim-d044); many
>   small cuts [D043](#claim-d043).

## Key takeaways
- **Thin, don't head:** thinning opens the canopy cleanly; heading breeds thorny water-sprouts
  and cuts the next crop [C078](#claim-c078), [C079](#claim-c079), [A081](#claim-a081).
- **Open the interior, keep the exterior dense** — the reconciliation of "prune for light"
  [A096](#claim-a096) vs "don't thin, prevent sunburn" [A123](#claim-a123); target an open-center/vase form [C099](#claim-c099).
- **Prune just after harvest** (preserves fruiting wood) and in late winter/early spring;
  **never late summer/fall or mid-summer heat** [A124](#claim-a124), [C101](#claim-c101), [C081](#claim-c081).
- **Pull rootstock suckers; manage water-sprouts selectively** [B130–B133]; suppress with a
  trunk collar [B134](#claim-b134).
- **Don't snip individual thorns** — remove whole branches in dormancy [C056](#claim-c056).
- **Sharp, sanitized tools, many small cuts** — clean cuts seal ~40% faster [D042](#claim-d042), [D044](#claim-d044), [D043](#claim-d043).
- Pruning shapes; **size control comes from the rootstock + root pruning** (§2, §5).

## Things not to do
- **Don't hard-head or heavily prune** a vigorous Lisbon — water-sprout thicket, less fruit
  [C079](#claim-c079), [A081](#claim-a081), [C080](#claim-c080).
- **Don't strip/over-thin the outer canopy** — bark sunburn on the exposed west side [A080](#claim-a080), [A123](#claim-a123), [B123](#claim-b123).
- **Don't prune in late summer/fall** (cold-tender flush, lost buds) or in mid-summer heat
  [C101](#claim-c101), [C081](#claim-c081).
- **Don't snip thorns individually** (infection entry) [C056](#claim-c056).
- **Don't disinfect the cut surface itself** [D045](#claim-d045); **don't use dull/dirty tools** [D042](#claim-d042).
- **Don't rely on pruning for size control** in place of a dwarfing rootstock [C079, §2].

## Evidence gaps
- **Lisbon-specific and container pruning advice is weak** (consumer/nursery) [C056](#claim-c056), [C001](#claim-c001), [D102](#claim-d102); no controlled trial on pruning a container Lisbon.
- **The exact "10–15%/yr" young-tree removal figure is a single extension rule of thumb**
  [A122](#claim-a122), not Lisbon-validated.
- **The open-vs-dense balance is qualitative** — no quantitative target for how much interior
  thinning is safe before sunburn risk rises [A096 vs A123].
- **Ramification/bonsai claims are weak** and explicitly trade away fruit [D059–D062].
