## Summary
UC IPM guidance on citrus leafminer (CLM), a near-universal southern-California pest whose larvae mine new leaves. Notable because coastal lemons with continual flushing — exactly Lisbon's habit — are the main exception to the "leave it alone" rule.

## Key Findings
- CLM larvae **mine serpentine tunnels in young leaves, causing curling/distortion**; it infests most of southern California and the coast.
- **In cool coastal areas the population can stay high from summer through fall.**
- **Usual advice is to tolerate the damage and let natural enemies (parasitoids) work** — established trees outgrow it.
- **Exception: coastal lemons with multiple flushes** can sustain heavier CLM pressure; on young trees this can slow establishment.
- Avoid insecticides that disrupt natural enemies; **don't prune off damaged leaves** (they still photosynthesize and host parasitoids).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Relevant but usually low-action. The balcony Lisbon's frequent coastal flushing makes it a CLM magnet, but mature trees tolerate the cosmetic damage — so the default is **monitor, don't spray, and don't remove mined leaves**. Pay closer attention only while the tree is young/establishing, when repeated flush damage can set back growth. CLM-driven leaf wounds can also be entry points worth noting alongside other stress.
