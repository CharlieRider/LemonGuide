## Summary
Characterizes the coastal-LA marine layer ("June Gloom") and its effect on light and humidity — the climate feature that both shields the Brentwood balcony from peak heat and raises foliar disease pressure. A double-edged microclimate factor for the tree.

## Key Findings
- The marine layer is a **low-cloud/fog inversion** strongest **May–June** (and on many summer mornings), formed by cool ocean air under subtropical high pressure.
- It **reduces morning daily light integral (DLI)** — overcast mornings cut photosynthetically active radiation until midday burn-off.
- It **raises humidity and prolongs leaf wetness** in the morning — favoring greasy spot, sooty mold, and other foliar fungal issues (Topic 8 link).
- It **moderates daytime high temperatures** near the coast, buffering heat stress relative to inland LA.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Net positive for heat, mild negative for disease/light. The marine layer means the Brentwood balcony rarely sees inland-level heat extremes (a benefit for root and canopy heat), but the citrus still needs the ≥6 h direct sun it gets after midday burn-off. The morning humidity argues for **soil-level (not overhead) watering done early so foliage dries**, and for keeping airflow — managing the fungal risk the marine layer raises while enjoying its heat buffering.
