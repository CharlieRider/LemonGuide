## Summary
Commercial agronomy reference on the role of calcium and magnesium in citrus and how to supply them. Included for nutrition targets only (per source-priority rules), to back the Ca/Mg amendment strategy that container leaching and coir use make necessary.

## Key Findings
- **Calcium** builds cell-wall strength, fruit firmness and rind quality, and supports root tip growth — deficiency shows as weak new growth and poor fruit integrity.
- **Magnesium** is the central atom of chlorophyll; deficiency produces interveinal chlorosis on **older** leaves (distinguishing it from Fe/Mn chlorosis on new growth).
- Ca and Mg are **basic cations easily leached** from a free-draining container and are **specifically depleted by unbuffered coir** (which is Na/K-loaded, Ca/Mg-poor).
- Supply options: gypsum or calcium nitrate (Ca without raising pH from gypsum; N+Ca from Ca-nitrate); dolomitic lime supplies Mg while raising pH; Epsom salt (MgSO₄) supplies Mg without pH change.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Practical. The 14" pot's leaching plus likely coir content makes Ca/Mg supplementation a planned input, not an afterthought: pair gypsum (Ca, pH-neutral) with a Mg source (dolomite if pH needs raising, Epsom salt if not). Old-leaf interveinal chlorosis here points to Mg, not iron — a useful field diagnostic.
