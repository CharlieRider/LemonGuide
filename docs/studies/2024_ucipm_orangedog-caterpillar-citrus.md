## Summary
UC IPM guidance on the orangedog — the large caterpillar (larva of the giant swallowtail butterfly, *Papilio cresphontes*) that chews citrus foliage. Mostly trivial on big trees, but a genuine threat to a small container tree it can defoliate quickly.

## Key Findings
- Orangedogs are **large, conspicuous caterpillars camouflaged as bird droppings** (brown/white, shiny); they eat citrus leaves and **a few larvae can defoliate a small or young tree fast**.
- Diagnose by the **caterpillars themselves plus chewed leaves and frass (droppings)**.
- **Hand-picking is the primary control** on small trees — just remove the (few, large, easy-to-see) larvae.
- For heavier pressure, **Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)** gives targeted caterpillar control while sparing bees, birds, and beneficial insects; use selective insecticides only if needed.
- The adult is a desirable native butterfly, so tolerate light feeding on established trees.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Genuinely important *because* the Lisbon is small: on a 14"-pot tree, **even a few orangedogs can strip a damaging fraction of the limited canopy**, so inspect the foliage regularly and **hand-pick the caterpillars** (they're large and obvious). Reserve **Bt** for an outbreak. No broad-spectrum spraying is warranted. Pairs with the leafminer (another lemon leaf pest) and IPM-treatment studies.
