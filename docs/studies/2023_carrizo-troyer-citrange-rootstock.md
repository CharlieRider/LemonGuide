## Summary
Profile of Carrizo and Troyer citrange (sweet orange × trifoliate hybrids) — among the most widely planted citrus rootstocks in California. The key takeaway for this project is mixed: good disease tolerance and fruit quality, but poor salinity/high-pH tolerance and **incompatibility with Eureka-type lemons**.

## Key Findings
- Carrizo and Troyer are **near-identical**; both are **reasonably vigorous**, giving **medium-to-large ("standard")** tree size — not dwarfing.
- **Disease/pest**: resistant to *Phytophthora parasitica*, citrus nematode, and tristeza virus (CTV); good internal fruit quality, medium-large fruit, early bearing.
- **Cold**: moderate cold tolerance (intermediate — less than trifoliate).
- **Weakness — salt & pH**: **sensitive to salinity** (rapidly accumulates toxic ions) and **intolerant of high-pH/high-lime soils** — a real liability with alkaline LA tap water.
- **Compatibility caution**: Carrizo is **not compatible with Eureka-type lemons**; lemon–citrange combinations need verification before relying on them.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Mixed fit for the Lisbon. Carrizo's CTV/Phytophthora resistance and good fruit quality are attractive, but two strikes matter here: it gives a **standard (large) tree** working against the manageable-container goal, and it is **salt- and high-pH-sensitive** — the opposite of what coastal-LA alkaline water calls for. If a Lisbon is found on Carrizo, expect to lean harder on root pruning for size and on salt-leaching for the root zone. A dwarfing trifoliate (Flying Dragon) or salt-tolerant lemon rootstock is generally a better container match. Pairs with the C-35, Flying Dragon, and rootstock-salinity studies.
