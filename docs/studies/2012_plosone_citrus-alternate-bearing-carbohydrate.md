## Summary
Explains **alternate (biennial) bearing** — the heavy-crop-year ("ON") followed by light-crop-year ("OFF") cycle — and its driver: carbohydrate reserves and crop load competing against next year's flower-bearing wood. Relevant because a small container tree is especially prone to swinging between gluts and gaps.

## Key Findings
- Alternate bearing = a heavy **ON** year followed by a sparse **OFF** year; caused chiefly by **reduced flowering in the spring after a heavy crop**.
- Carbohydrate reserves are **inversely related to crop load**: a heavy fruit set diverts carbohydrate into fruit instead of new vegetative flush — leaving **less new wood to flower** the next season.
- A heavy fruit crop also **suppresses summer/fall vegetative shoots** (the shoots that would carry next year's bloom), reinforcing the OFF year.
- Gene-expression work confirms ON vs. OFF trees differ in flowering-control gene expression, not just resources.
- Management: moderate the crop (thinning) and protect late-season vegetative flushes to dampen the swing.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A real risk for the Lisbon: a small root/canopy volume has limited carbohydrate capacity, so a bumper crop one year can starve the next year's bloom and growth. Practical container tactics — **thin an excessive fruit set**, feed steadily (per the N-rate study), and avoid letting one heavy crop monopolize the tree — keep production even year to year. Note lemons everbearing habit softens this somewhat vs. mandarins. Connects to the fruit-drop, pruning, and nutrition studies.
