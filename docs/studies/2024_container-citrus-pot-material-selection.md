## Summary
Compares pot materials for a tree meant to live a decade in a container, weighing root health, thermal behavior, weight, and durability. The pot is a long-term decision the other lifecycle studies (repotting, root pruning, decline) all build on.

## Key Findings
- **Unglazed terracotta**: porous walls **breathe** (aerate the root zone) and buffer temperature, but **dry out fast, are heavy, and crack** (especially in cold) — high maintenance for a thirsty pot.
- **Glazed ceramic**: sealed walls **hold moisture** and look good, but heavy and breakable.
- **Plastic**: light and cheap but **heats up in sun** (roots circling the hot wall suffer drought/heat/disease) and **warps/fades/cracks** in UV over years.
- **Fabric (air-pruning)**: excellent drainage and aeration, **air-prunes circling roots**, lightweight — but **dries out fastest** (a liability on a hot balcony) and is less durable/attractive long-term.
- **Wood**: slow to dry, insulating, frost-resistant, moderate weight.
- For citrus, a **~15-gal-class container is the practical "sweet spot"** — big enough for healthy roots, light enough for one person to move; moderate root restriction actually improves fruit-per-canopy.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Frames the trade-offs for the Lisbon's 19"×14" pot (≈15-gal class — the recommended size). On a hot west-facing balcony, the key tension is **aeration vs. dry-down**: fabric/terracotta aerate (good for the shallow perched-water zone) but dry too fast in the sun; sealed glazed/plastic hold water but heat up (mitigate with light color, double-potting, mulch — see container-color and heat studies). A **light-colored glazed or double-potted plastic** balances moisture retention with manageable root temperature; pair with a coarse mix to keep aeration. Connects to the container-color, air-pruning, root-restriction (Topic 1), and repotting studies.
