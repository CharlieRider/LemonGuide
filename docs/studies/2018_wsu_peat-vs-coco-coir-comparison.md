## Summary
A comparison of the two dominant organic base components — sphagnum peat moss and coconut coir — across the physical and chemical properties that matter for choosing the water-holding fraction of a citrus mix. Relevant because the choice sets baseline pH, salt load, and rewetting behavior before any aggregate is added.

## Key Findings
- **Water holding:** peat ~**60–68%** of volume; coir ~**73–80%** — coir holds somewhat more water at saturation.
- **pH:** peat is strongly acidic (**3.0–4.5**, usually limed before sale); coir is near-ideal (**5.8–6.9**) and typically needs no liming.
- **Rewetting:** coir is **easier to rehydrate and far less hydrophobic** than dried peat (consistent with the 2014 hydration study) — a practical advantage for a pot that dries hard.
- **Salts:** raw coir carries **Na, Cl, and K**; quality coir is buffered/soaked in Ca–Mg (e.g., calcium nitrate) to displace Na/K — unbuffered coir can deliver a salt and high-K load.
- **Caution for citrus:** a 2005 Utah State study found mixes with **>50% coir reduced plant growth** vs. peat controls, tied to high K, low Ca, and phenolics in poorly processed coir. Coir is inert (no nutrients) — all feeding must come from fertilizer.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Informs the base-component choice for the Lisbon's mix. Coir's higher pH, easy rewetting, and renewability favor it over peat for a fast-drying balcony pot — but keep it a **minority fraction (well under 50%)**, use buffered coir, and supply calcium (the K/Ca imbalance and salts are the real risks for citrus). Connects to the coco-coir buffering study already in the repo, the gypsum/Ca study, and the hydration/wetting-agent study.
