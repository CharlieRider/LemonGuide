## Summary
Addresses a container-specific heat source the orchard studies miss: **reflected and re-radiated heat from balcony hardscape** — walls, concrete floors, railings, and glass — which can push a potted tree's effective heat load far above the air temperature. Critical for a west-facing balcony in summer.

## Key Findings
- A **bare concrete/masonry wall in sun can run 20–40°F hotter than ambient air**, then **re-radiate that heat** to nearby objects (including a pot and canopy) for hours after sunset.
- Hard surfaces create a **localized urban-heat-island microclimate**: a balcony can be substantially hotter than the regional forecast, with heat hitting the plant from the side (wall) and below (floor) as well as overhead.
- **Reflective light-colored surfaces lower their own surface temperature but can increase radiant-heat *exposure*** of objects in front of them — a white wall bounces more light/heat onto the plant.
- **Vegetation/transpiration cools the microclimate**: foliage covering a wall can drop balcony temperatures by **up to ~10°F** by intercepting solar load and transpiring.
- The pot itself, sitting on hot hardscape, gains conductive + radiant heat at the root zone (compounding the container-color effect).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A major, often-overlooked stressor for the Lisbon: the west-facing wall and concrete floor can make the balcony microclimate much hotter than the LA forecast and heat the pot from multiple directions, worsening root-zone heating (Topic 1) and canopy/fruit sunburn. Mitigations: **lift the pot off hot concrete** (feet/caster, a light-colored tile, or a plant stand), **shade or screen the west wall**, keep the pot light-colored/double-potted/mulched, and avoid placing it directly against a sun-baked masonry surface. Ties to the container-color, mulch/double-pot, shade-cloth, and root-zone-temperature studies.
