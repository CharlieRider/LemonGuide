## Summary
An extension guide on raising and lowering the pH of soilless container substrates — the management lever that controls micronutrient availability. Bark-based mixes start acidic and drift, while alkaline tap water pushes pH up over time; this gives the materials and rough rates to correct both directions.

## Key Findings
- **Target for container citrus: pH ~6.0–7.0** (IFAS); moving a substrate from >7 down to **6.0–6.5** increases availability of K, P, Ca, Mn, Zn and Fe — the nutrients citrus most often locks out at high pH.
- **To lower pH:** elemental sulfur — about **1 teaspoon of 90% sulfur prills per #1 (1-gal) container drops pH ~1 unit** (e.g., 7.5→6.5); incorporate **1–2 lb of 90% sulfur per cubic yard** at potting for chronically high pH. Acid injection (phosphoric/sulfuric) or ammoniacal-N fertilizers also acidify over time.
- **To raise pH:** liming, where effectiveness depends on material (calcitic vs. dolomitic limestone, hydrated lime), particle size (fine reacts faster), and rate (≈1,200–5,000 lb/acre equivalent); **~1 tablespoon dolomitic lime per gallon of mix** is a common rate to buffer raw bark.
- pH is dynamic in containers — irrigation-water alkalinity and fertilizer chemistry move it continuously, so it must be monitored, not set once.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly actionable. Brentwood tap water is alkaline, so a 14" citrus pot will trend toward high pH and iron/manganese chlorosis over a season. This study gives the correction kit: a touch of dolomite to buffer fresh bark at potting, then small sulfur additions or acidifying fertilizer to hold the root zone near 6.0–6.5. Pair with the micronutrient-deficiency, chelate-selection, and biochar (pH-raising) studies.
