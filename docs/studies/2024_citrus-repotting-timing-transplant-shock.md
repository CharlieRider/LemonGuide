## Summary
Covers *when* in the year to repot a citrus and how to minimize and recover from transplant shock — the timing-and-aftercare layer that complements the existing "repotting technique & size increase" entry (which covers the mechanics).

## Key Findings
- **Best time = spring to early summer**, when the tree is entering active growth — it gets the whole season to re-establish; **avoid winter repotting** (slow recovery).
- A good window is **just after harvest, before the next bloom** — repotting a tree that's about to bloom risks it dropping the flowers from combined stress.
- **Transplant shock lasts ~1–2 weeks**: expect some leaf drop/yellowing/wilting; this is normal recovery, not failure.
- **Aftercare**: water in thoroughly to settle the mix and eliminate air pockets; then **hold off fertilizer ~4–6 weeks**; keep the tree **out of harsh direct sun** and stable temperatures for ~1 week, then return it to full light and normal feeding.
- **Keep the root ball largely intact** for fastest recovery (unless deliberately root-pruning to hold size — a separate, more stressful operation).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Sets the schedule for the Lisbon's periodic repotting/media refresh: do it in **spring (or right after a harvest, before bloom)**, not in winter or during a heat wave. After repotting, **shade the tree for a week, hold fertilizer 4–6 weeks**, and water carefully — and don't panic at some leaf drop. If combining with root pruning to hold size (see that study), expect more shock and recover accordingly. Pairs with the repotting-technique, root-pruning, sun-acclimation, and care-calendar studies.
