## Summary
Foundational lab study quantifying the lethal high-temperature thresholds of excised citrus rootstock roots using electrolyte-leakage injury assays. Establishes the root-zone temperature ceiling that container thermal management must stay below — directly extrapolable physiology even though the work was not container-specific.

## Key Findings
- **Critical (lethal) temperature for a 20-minute exposure: 51.6°C (Carrizo citrange), 52.5°C (sour orange), 53.5°C (Swingle citrumelo)** — roots, by electrolyte-leakage injury. (~125–128°F)
- **Predicted critical exposure duration at 50°C ≈ 45 minutes** for citrus before membrane injury.
- In a companion 6-hr/day growth regime at 28/34/40°C, **34°C (93°F) was the maximum allowable / optimum** root temperature; 40°C suppressed growth.
- Damage is a dose of temperature × time: brief excursions to ~50°C are survivable, but sustained elevated root temperature degrades growth long before the acute lethal threshold.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Critical reference. Sun-exposed dark container walls on a west balcony can drive media temperatures past 50°C; this study shows ~45 min at 50°C begins killing roots and that anything sustained above ~34°C is already harmful. It sets the hard target for pot color, double-potting, and mulch decisions in Topic 9.
