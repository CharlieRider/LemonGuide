## Summary
UC IPM guidance on citrus thrips, a tiny rasping insect that scars young fruit rind. Cosmetic for home use but worth recognizing so a balcony grower doesn't mistake the ring-scar for disease.

## Key Findings
- Thrips puncture rind epidermal cells, leaving **scabby grayish/silvery scars**; as fruit expands the damage forms a characteristic **"ring scar"** around the stem end.
- **Fruit are susceptible from petal fall until ~1.5 inch (3.7 cm) diameter** — a narrow window; older fruit and foliage damage are cosmetic and don't reduce fruit quality/yield meaningfully.
- Monitor susceptible fruit **twice weekly after petal fall**; examine undersides of 5-leaf terminals from shady canopy and note predatory mites (natural control).
- No rigid numeric threshold (varies by variety, region, beneficial mites); presence at petal fall on susceptible varieties can warrant action.
- Treatments that spare natural enemies: spinetoram, spinosad, abamectin; avoid broad-spectrum sprays that flare mites.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Low priority for a backyard Lisbon — thrips damage is **cosmetic rind scarring**, not a health threat, and home lemons aren't graded for appearance. If scarring is unwanted, the only window that matters is **petal fall to small-fruit (~1.5")**; a spinosad spray then is sufficient. Don't spray broadly for thrips — it kills the predatory mites that keep spider mites in check (see spider-mite study). Pairs with the spider-mite, bud/rust-mite, and IPM-treatment studies.
