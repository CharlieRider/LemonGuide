## Summary
Explains why a container citrus must be **gradually acclimated** to a new light/heat exposure rather than placed straight into full sun — and how to do it. Specific to potted trees, which (unlike planted ones) get moved between environments and can be shocked.

## Key Findings
- A tree grown in shade/greenhouse/indoors will **sunburn and drop leaves if moved abruptly into full sun**; acclimation hardens the foliage to the new light intensity.
- **Acclimate over ~2–3 weeks**: start in bright shade / 1–2 hours of direct sun, then increase exposure gradually before placing in the final spot.
- During the adjustment, **protect the tree if temperatures go below ~40°F or above ~85°F**, and **shade above 95–100°F**.
- Expect **some leaf drop** during the transition — normal; new leaves emerge adapted to the higher light.
- The same principle applies seasonally and after heavy pruning (newly exposed interior leaves/bark can burn — see bark-sunburn study).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly practical for the Lisbon whenever it changes environment: a **newly purchased or relocated tree** (e.g., nursery → west-facing balcony, or moved indoors for a cold snap and back out) should be **re-introduced to full afternoon sun over 2–3 weeks**, not placed straight into the harsh west exposure, to avoid mass leaf burn/drop. Likewise, harden it back up gradually after any indoor cold-protection stint. Pairs with the freeze-threshold, shade-cloth, bark-sunburn (pruning), and heat-wave-protocol studies.
