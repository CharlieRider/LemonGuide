## Summary
The cold-side counterpart to the heat studies: the **critical temperatures** at which citrus tissues freeze, the **order of susceptibility**, and where lemons fall on the cold-hardiness scale. Relevant even in mild LA because a rare cold snap, amplified on an exposed balcony, can damage a tender lemon.

## Key Findings
- **Susceptibility order (most → least sensitive): flowers > vegetative flush > fruit > hardened leaves > woody tissue** — new growth and bloom are lost first; trunks/branches survive longest.
- **Lemons and limes are among the least cold-hardy citrus** (cold-hardiness ranking: satsuma > mandarin > sweet orange > grapefruit > lemon/lime); lemons are **killed or severely damaged in the mid-to-high 20s °F**.
- **Fruit freezes at ~26–28°F** when held there several hours; **fruit is slightly less cold-tolerant than hardened leaves**, and a fruit-laden tree is less tolerant than a bare one.
- Comparators: Meyer lemon critical ~22°F; mandarins tolerate ~20°F/4 h before serious wood damage — true lemons are more tender than these.
- Damage depends on **duration and how hardened the tree is**, not just the minimum reached.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Coastal Brentwood rarely freezes, but the Lisbon is a tender (mid-20s°F) lemon and a container's root zone and canopy are **more exposed than an in-ground tree** (no soil thermal buffer). On a forecast cold night, the easy container advantages apply: **move the pot against the warm house wall, under eaves, or indoors**, and protect fruit/new flush first (the most sensitive tissue). Mostly a few-nights-per-year concern here. Complements the existing frost/cold-protection (practical) and container-color/radiant-heat studies; mirrors the high-temperature fruit-set susceptibility order.
