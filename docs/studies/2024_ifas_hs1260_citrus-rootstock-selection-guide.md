## Summary
The umbrella reference for choosing a citrus rootstock, plus the California home-garden cold-hardiness rankings. It frames the trade space the individual-rootstock studies in this topic sit within: size control, disease tolerance, soil/salt adaptation, and cold hardiness all move together with rootstock choice.

## Key Findings
- Rootstock controls scion vigor/size, precocity, fruit quality, cold hardiness, and tolerance to Phytophthora, nematodes, CTV (tristeza), salinity, and high-pH soils — no single rootstock wins on all axes.
- **Cold-hardiness ranking (induced in scion)**: most cold-tolerant on **trifoliate orange, sour orange, Cleopatra mandarin, Swingle citrumelo**; **intermediate on Carrizo citrange and sweet orange**; vigorous lemon rootstocks (Volkamer, rough lemon, macrophylla) impart the **least** cold tolerance.
- **The only true dwarfing rootstock is Flying Dragon** (trifoliate selection), yielding a ~4–8 ft tree — the rest are standard-to-semi-dwarf.
- UC Master Gardener guidance: Carrizo gives high-quality fruit (later season) and is used in CA; for a manageable backyard/container tree, dwarfing trifoliate types are preferred.
- Practical selection = match rootstock to the *limiting* local stress (here: heat, alkaline/salty water, container size, mild winters).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
This is the decision framework for the Lisbon: in coastal LA, winter cold is a minor constraint, so the priorities become **size control (Flying Dragon / dwarfing trifoliate), Phytophthora tolerance, and salt/high-pH adaptation** — exactly the axes the per-rootstock studies in this topic detail. Vigor-boosting lemon rootstocks (Volkamer, macrophylla) fight the goal of a manageable 14"-pot tree even though they tolerate salt well. Anchors the Flying Dragon, C-35, Carrizo, Swingle, Volkamer, and Alemow entries.
