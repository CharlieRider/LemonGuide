## Summary
Shows that root-zone flooding does not just starve citrus roots of oxygen — it directly cripples iron acquisition, linking the wet-pot hypoxia problem to the chlorosis commonly blamed on pH or fertilizer. A bridge between Topic 1 (root-zone oxygen) and Topic 3 (micronutrients).

## Key Findings
- Flooding **strongly down-regulates Strategy I iron-acquisition genes** (ferric-chelate reductase FRO, root H⁺-ATPase, Fe transporter IRT) in citrus roots, impairing Fe uptake and distribution.
- Result: interveinal **chlorosis under waterlogging is partly an induced Fe-uptake failure**, not only an oxygen-deprivation symptom — so foliar/soil iron will not fully correct chlorosis while roots remain hypoxic.
- Confirms citrus's Strategy-I (dicot-type) Fe response is suppressed precisely when the root zone is oxygen-limited.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly relevant diagnostically. A shallow pot that stays saturated after winter rain or overwatering can produce yellowing that mimics iron deficiency; this study shows the real fix is restoring root-zone oxygen (drainage/aeration), after which Fe uptake recovers — not piling on more chelate.
