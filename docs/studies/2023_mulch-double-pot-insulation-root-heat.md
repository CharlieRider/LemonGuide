## Summary
Synthesizes practical insulation tactics — surface mulch, double-potting, and pot shading — against the root-zone heat that the citrus root-death thresholds (Topic 1) and container-color data (Topic 9) show is the limiting stress in a shallow west-facing pot.

## Key Findings
- **Surface mulch/topdress** shades the substrate top and cuts evaporative drying and solar heating of the upper root zone; also moderates temperature swings.
- **Double-potting (pot-in-pot with an air gap)** insulates the inner root ball from direct solar load on the outer wall — the air gap blocks conductive heat transfer.
- **Shading the sun-facing pot wall** (or grouping pots) directly cuts the ~50°C sidewall temperatures that black pots reach (Topic 9 container-color study).
- These stack with **light pot color** for the largest combined root-zone cooling, keeping the root zone below the **>38°C growth-stop / ~50°C injury** range for citrus (Topic 1).
- Mulch must be kept off the trunk/graft to avoid crown rot (Topic 8).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Core heat toolkit for the shallow pot. Because a 14"-deep pot has a large surface-to-volume ratio, the root zone heats fast — so layer the defenses: **light-colored pot + double-pot (air gap) + surface mulch + shade the west-facing wall.** Together these can keep the root zone out of the growth-arrest/injury range during summer afternoons. Keep mulch clear of the crown.
