## Summary
Cold-side environmental stress: lemons are among the most cold-sensitive citrus, and container trees are more exposed than in-ground ones. Defines the LA cold-snap risk and the simple protections that cover it.

## Key Findings
- **Lemons/limes/citrons are the most cold-sensitive citrus** — damaged or killed at **mid-to-high 20s °F (~-2 to -4°C)**; oranges/grapefruit tolerate a few degrees lower; mandarins lowest.
- **Fruit freezes at 26–28°F sustained for several hours** (e.g. 4 h at 26°F ≈ 30% fruit damage in oranges).
- **Container and young trees are more frost-susceptible** because roots are above ground and exposed.
- Protections: **cover with frost cloth/burlap to the ground to trap soil heat, water the soil beforehand (moist soil holds heat), and site near a south/west wall** for reradiated heat.
- Newly flushed tender growth is the most cold-vulnerable — avoid late-season feeding that pushes soft growth into winter (Topic 3 link).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Occasional but real risk in Brentwood. Coastal LA rarely hits the low-to-mid 20s°F, but radiative cold nights can, and a container lemon's exposed root zone makes it more vulnerable than an in-ground tree. On a forecast cold snap: **move the pot against the building wall, water beforehand, and drape frost cloth to the floor.** The west-facing wall's reradiated heat is a built-in advantage. Avoid late-fall nitrogen so the tree isn't carrying tender growth into a cold night.
