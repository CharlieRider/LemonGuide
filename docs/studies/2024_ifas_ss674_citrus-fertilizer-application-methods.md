## Summary
The IFAS chapter comparing how fertilizer is delivered — dry granular, fertigation (liquid through irrigation), controlled-release, and foliar — with the tradeoffs of each. For a container grower the question isn't acreage equipment but which delivery mode wastes least and matches a small, fast-leaching root volume.

## Key Findings
- **Fertigation** places nutrients directly in the wetted active-root zone, allows frequent small doses, raises efficiency and **reduces leaching** — IFAS notes "similar or better growth, yield, and quality with *less* fertilizer" vs. conventional methods.
- Keep fertigation solution **EC below ~1.5 dS/m (~1000 ppm TDS)** to avoid salt injury; for young trees, minimize post-injection irrigation so nutrients aren't flushed past the roots.
- **Foliar** application gives rapid uptake and is especially effective for micronutrients (Zn, Mn, Cu, B, Mg) and when roots can't take up nutrients (cold, saturated, or high-pH soil) — but its effect is **temporary (one flush)** and it cannot replace soil N-P-K. Spray cool/humid hours, target 2/3-to-fully-expanded young leaves, keep solution **pH 6–7** (urea above pH 7 risks ammonia leaf burn).
- **Dry granular** is cheapest but prone to loss and uneven placement; **controlled-release** needs the fewest applications (≥1/yr).
- Placement near active roots is critical regardless of method.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Maps cleanly onto a balcony pot: the lowest-waste options are **controlled-release prills** (fewest applications, steady feed) or **dilute liquid fertigation in many small doses** (mimicking the ≥10×/yr cadence), both keeping solution EC well under 1.5 dS/m to protect the confined root zone. Reserve foliar micronutrient sprays for correcting Fe/Zn/Mn chlorosis fast — knowing the effect lasts only one flush. Pairs with the CRF/N-leaching, salt/EC, and foliar-micronutrient studies.
