## Summary
A lemon-specific (and Lisbon-specific) study of how lemons set fruit: their degree of self-fertility, cross-compatibility, and **parthenocarpy** (fruit without pollination). Directly answers the practical balcony question — does a lone potted Lisbon need a pollinator partner?

## Key Findings
- **Lemons are largely self-fertile** and set fruit alone — a single tree fruits without a second variety nearby.
- **Parthenocarpy** (seedless set without pollination) is **low in most lemons, but Lisbon has the greatest parthenocarpic capacity (~20% fruit set)** — Lisbon can set a meaningful crop with no pollination at all.
- Most cultivars were **moderately self-compatible (20–40% fruit set)** when self-pollinated; cross-pollination gave similar rates — i.e., a second variety adds little.
- **Pollinators still help**: open (insect-accessible) vs. closed flowers raised fruit set by **~44%** — bees boost yield even though they aren't strictly required.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Reassuring for a solitary balcony Lisbon: **no companion tree is needed** — it is self-fertile and, uniquely among lemons, sets a fair fraction of fruit parthenocarpically (≈20%). To maximize yield, encourage bee access during bloom (the ~44% pollinator boost) rather than worrying about a second variety. A west-facing balcony with some pollinator traffic is sufficient. Pairs with the fruit-set physiology, bloom-flush, and yield-expectations studies.
