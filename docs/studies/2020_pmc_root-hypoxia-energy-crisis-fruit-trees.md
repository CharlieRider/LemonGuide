## Summary
Review of how fruit trees respond to root-zone oxygen deficit, with the air-filled-porosity thresholds that define when a substrate becomes hypoxic. This supplies the "how wet is too wet" number that pairs with the container-depth porosity data to explain shallow-pot root failure.

## Key Findings
- **Below ~12% air-filled porosity**, root respiration and O₂/CO₂ exchange become impaired (Cook & Knight 2003).
- **Below ~10% AFP**, hypoxia is triggered; more severe saturation produces anoxia (Kawase).
- General requirement: **≥10% of soil volume as gas-filled pores at field capacity, with ≥10% of that gas being O₂** for normal root function.
- Citrus is **flood-sensitive**: hypoxia causes chlorosis, root rot, retarded development and excessive fruit abscission; tolerant genotypes merely *delay* oxidative damage (MDA accumulation, SOD/APX/CAT response) rather than avoiding it.
- Energy deficit of **3–37.5%** in ATP formation occurs as O₂ limits cytochrome-oxidase activity.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Central to Topic 1. The 10–12% AFP hypoxia threshold is the line a 14"-deep container's mix must stay above in its saturated bottom zone. Combined with the container-depth porosity study, it explains why a peat-heavy mix in a shallow pot drowns roots: the perched-water zone sits below 10% AFP and citrus, being flood-sensitive, declines rather than adapts.
