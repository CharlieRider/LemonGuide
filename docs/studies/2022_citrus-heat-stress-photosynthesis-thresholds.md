## Summary
Establishes the canopy (leaf/air) temperature thresholds where citrus photosynthesis and fruiting begin to fail — the above-ground counterpart to the root-zone thresholds. Defines when the west-balcony heat load is hurting the tree.

## Key Findings
- **Optimum growth/photosynthesis 25–30°C**; citrus grows across ~10–35°C.
- **Mild heat stress begins at ~36°C leaf temperature**; leaves can run well above air temperature on sunny days.
- **Photosynthesis declines progressively above ~30°C (86°F)**, before any visible symptoms.
- **38–40°C exposure cut net photosynthesis 39.8% (navel) to 55.6% (satsuma) after 25 days** (PMID 12973985).
- Visible progression: **inward leaf-edge curl → leaf bleaching/yellowing → reduced flowering/fruit set, increased fruit drop, fruit sunburn**.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Sets the canopy-side heat targets. Brentwood's coastal moderation usually keeps air below the 36°C mild-stress point, but west-facing radiant load and occasional heat waves will exceed it — cutting photosynthesis and risking fruit drop/sunburn. Combine canopy defenses (afternoon shade cloth on hot days, keep canopy dense for self-shading) with the pot-color/root cooling from the container-color study. Leaf-edge curl is the early field cue to intervene.
