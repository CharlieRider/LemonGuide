## Summary
A practical reference on organic (natural-source) fertilizers for citrus and their nutrient analyses and release behavior — relevant for a container grower who wants a slower, gentler, lower-salt feeding program than synthetic soluble salts, or who simply prefers organic inputs.

## Key Findings
- **Feather meal ≈ 12-0-0** — a high-N, slow-release organic source that mineralizes over weeks and also feeds soil fungi; good base N for a long, even feed.
- **Fish emulsion ≈ 2-4-1** plus Ca, Mg, S and trace elements — fast-acting liquid; dilute and apply **every 2–4 weeks during active growth** for container plants.
- **Compost / worm castings / well-rotted manure** supply slow-release N, micronutrients, and microbes, and improve water retention and drainage in the mix.
- **General rule for organics**: meals (feather, blood, bone) for long-term steady feeding; liquids (fish, seaweed) for quick uptake — combine for baseline + responsiveness.
- Organic sources are generally **lower salt index and lower burn risk** than concentrated soluble synthetics, but their N release is **temperature- and microbe-dependent**, so it slows in cool media (relevant given citrus roots barely grow below ~22°C).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A workable organic program for the Lisbon: a slow meal (e.g., feather meal) worked into the top of the mix for steady N, supplemented by dilute fish/seaweed liquid every few weeks in the warm season. The low-salt nature suits a small pot prone to EC buildup, but because organic N release depends on warm, biologically active media, expect feeding to taper naturally in the cool months — match the seasonal pattern rather than forcing winter growth. Note: these are horticultural/commercial references, not peer-reviewed; cross-check rates against the IFAS per-tree N targets. Pairs with the N-rate, salt/EC, and root-zone-temperature studies.
