## Summary
A profile of how a Lisbon tree actually grows — its vigor, form, thorniness, and where it sets fruit — the morphology that drives the size-control and thorn-management work a container Lisbon needs.

## Key Findings
- **The most vigorous lemon grown in California** and the **most resistant to adverse conditions** (frost, heat, wind, neglect) — toughness is the cultivar's signature.
- Habit: **large, upright-spreading, densely foliated, and very thorny**; mature in-ground size **~15–20 ft tall × 10–15 ft wide**.
- **Fruit is borne largely inside the canopy** (not on the periphery like Eureka) — a consequence of the dense, upright form; this affects harvest access and interior-light pruning.
- **Thornier than Eureka**; thorns are most pronounced on vigorous young/water-sprout growth.
- **Clonal variation**: California Lisbon clones differ in vigor, size, thorn abundance, branching compactness, and foliage density (see clonal-strains study) — some are more compact/less thorny than others.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
This is *why* a container Lisbon is more work than a Meyer: its **natural vigor and dense, thorny, upright habit fight the 14"-pot goal**, so it relies heavily on a **size-controlling dwarf rootstock (Flying Dragon) plus regular thinning/size-control pruning and thorn management** to stay manageable. The interior fruiting habit also argues for opening the canopy (selective pruning) for light and harvest access. Choosing a **more compact, less thorny clone** at purchase helps. Directly connects to the size-control pruning, thorn-management, selective-pruning, rootstock, and clonal-strains studies.
