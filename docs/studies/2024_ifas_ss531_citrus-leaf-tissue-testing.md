## Summary
The IFAS protocol for diagnosing citrus nutrition by **leaf tissue analysis** — the standard way to know whether a tree is actually getting what the fertilizer label promises. It defines how/when to sample and the sufficiency ranges used to interpret results, the missing diagnostic layer for a container grower guessing at deficiencies.

## Key Findings
- **Sample the right leaves**: mature, non-fruiting **spring-flush leaves 4–6 months old**, because nutrient levels are most stable in that window (N declines with leaf age; P and K decline with age too). Avoid immature leaves.
- Collect **20–30 leaves** per sampling area to average out variation.
- **Leaf sufficiency (optimum) ranges** (% dry weight): N ~**2.5–2.7%**, P ~**0.12–0.16%**, K ~**1.2–1.7%** (deficient below ~0.7%), Ca ~**3.0–4.9%**, Mg ~**0.30–0.49%**; micronutrients in ppm.
- Combining leaf analysis with soil/media testing is what separates a real deficiency from a pH-induced lockout (e.g., adequate soil Fe but chlorotic leaves at high pH).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
This is the feedback loop for the whole nutrition topic: rather than over- or under-feeding a Lisbon by eye, pull a 20–30-leaf sample of 4–6-month spring-flush leaves once or twice a year and check against the sufficiency ranges. In coastal LA's alkaline-water context it's the way to catch the common container failure — normal N/K but low Fe/Mn/Zn from high pH — and decide between a foliar spray, a chelate, or a pH correction. Anchors the N-rate, potassium, micronutrient, and pH-management studies.
