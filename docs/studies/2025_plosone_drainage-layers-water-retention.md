## Summary
A controlled experiment testing the popular belief that a layer of gravel/grit/sand at the bottom of a pot "improves drainage." It does not. Across three media and several drainage-layer materials and thicknesses, a bottom layer **reduced or, at best, maintained** total water-holding capacity (WHC) — never increased it — and in organic media it pushed the perched water table *up into the root zone*. Critical Topic-1 myth-buster for anyone tempted to add gravel to a shallow citrus pot.

## Key Findings
- In a coir–perlite–bark (CPB) loamless mix, **every** drainage-layer condition gave significantly **lower** WHC than no layer (p < 0.001) — a drainage layer made the media *drier overall*, not wetter, by raising the saturated zone above the layer.
- The perched (saturated) zone sits at the bottom of the *fine* medium regardless of what is beneath it; adding a coarse layer simply lifts that saturated band higher into the pot, shortening the well-aerated rooting depth.
- **Thicker layers (60 mm) reduced retention more than thinner (30 mm)**; a 60 mm coarse-sand layer was the most universally effective at reducing retention across all media.
- Effect depended on medium: minimal in a loam-based John Innes mix; large in loamless organic mixes — and a fine Leca-30 mm layer in loam *increased* WHC (p < 0.05), showing the practice is unpredictable.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant and corrective. In a 14"-deep pot the perched water table already eats a disproportionate share of depth; adding gravel at the bottom (a common "balcony pot" habit) lifts the saturated zone even higher and shrinks the aerated root volume the Lisbon needs. The fix is a uniformly coarse bark/pumice mix top-to-bottom — not a drainage layer. Reinforces the container-height/AFP findings already in the repo.
