## Summary
Addresses the direct consequence of pruning a thin-barked citrus in a hot, sunny site: **bark sunburn/sunscald** on newly exposed trunk and scaffold limbs, and how to prevent it. Especially relevant for a west-facing balcony where afternoon sun is intense and lemons are the most sunburn-prone citrus.

## Key Findings
- Citrus has **paper-thin bark (~1 mm)** and is among the most sunburn-susceptible trees; **lemons are the most vulnerable** type.
- Sunburn strikes bark suddenly exposed by **pruning** (or by leaf loss), in summer heat; sunscald can also occur from winter sun on cold bark.
- **White latex paint is the standard sunscreen**: **water-based** (never oil-based) interior white latex, **diluted ~1:1 with water**, painted over all exposed non-green bark down to the base.
- **Best prevention is not over-pruning**: maintain a full canopy so foliage shades the trunk/scaffolds naturally — citrus naturally grows as a shrubby thicket that self-shades its bark.
- Alternatives: wrap exposed bark with **burlap, shade cloth, or tree wrap**.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A real and easy-to-miss risk for a west-facing-balcony Lisbon: any pruning that opens the canopy can expose trunk/branch bark to brutal afternoon sun, causing sunscald that invites borers and dieback. Practical rules — **prune conservatively** (favor thinning that retains some shading foliage), and **whitewash newly exposed trunk/scaffolds with 1:1 white latex** after any significant pruning.
