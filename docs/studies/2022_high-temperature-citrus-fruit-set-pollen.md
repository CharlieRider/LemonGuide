## Summary
Examines how heat at bloom and fruit set reduces yield — chiefly by damaging pollen and triggering flower/fruitlet drop. Connects the heat-stress topic to the fruiting topic: a heat wave during bloom can cost the crop even if the tree looks fine.

## Key Findings
- **High temperature reduces pollen viability and pollen-tube growth**, which is directly correlated with **reduced fruit set and yield** — pollen development is among the most heat-sensitive processes.
- Heat also **promotes flower and young-fruitlet drop** (abscission), leaf rolling, leaf senescence, and shoot/root growth inhibition.
- Reproductive tissues are the most heat-sensitive in the tree (flowers > vegetative flush > fruit > hardened leaves > wood — mirrors the cold-susceptibility order).
- Mitigations: **temperature management (shade, cooling), parthenocarpy** (set without pollination), heat-tolerant cultivars, and **anti-abscission PGRs** (synthetic auxins/cytokinins) that hold reproductive structures despite stress.
- Heat can also cause **fruit discoloration** and quality loss.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Explains why a hot spell during the Lisbon's bloom can cut fruit set, and why shading/cooling at bloom protects yield. Two silver linings for this tree: Lisbon's relatively **high parthenocarpy (~20%, see pollination study)** lets it set some fruit even if heat wrecks pollen, and a container can be **moved or shaded** during a bloom-time heat wave far more easily than an orchard tree. Avoid heat-stacking the tree at bloom/fruit set (shade, cool, steady water). Pairs with the pollination, fruit-drop, shade-cloth, and heat-wave-protocol studies.
