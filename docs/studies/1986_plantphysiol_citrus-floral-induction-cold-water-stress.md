## Summary
The classic study establishing the **two triggers of citrus bloom** — a cool period or a water-deficit period — and the key insight that *induction* and *flower emergence* are separated in time. This is the physiological "why citrus blooms when it does," foundational for managing bloom in a mild, manipulable container.

## Key Findings
- Citrus flowering is induced by **low temperature (autumn/winter, below ~15°C / 59°F)** OR by **water-deficit stress** — either can do it.
- **Induction precedes sprouting by ~2–3 months**; flower differentiation then occurs at bud sprout, not at induction (unlike deciduous fruit). The bud "decides" to be floral months before you see the flower.
- Water-deficit induction works similarly to cold: **40–60 days of moderate drought** raised the leaf florigen gene (CiFT3) like cold did — but the floral identity genes (LFY, AP1) **only expressed once the stress was relieved by rewatering** ("stress-then-release" triggers the actual bloom).
- More/longer stress → stronger flowering response.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly exploitable: a container Lisbon in mild coastal LA gets weak natural cold induction, so its **multiple bloom flushes are often triggered by dry-down-then-water cycles** — the everyday reality of pot watering. A controlled moderate dry spell followed by thorough watering can deliberately push a bloom flush; conversely, erratic drought may scatter flowering across the year. Explains why container lemons bloom repeatedly and how watering rhythm shapes it. Pairs with the Lisbon bloom-flush, improving-flowering (HS1399), and watering studies.
