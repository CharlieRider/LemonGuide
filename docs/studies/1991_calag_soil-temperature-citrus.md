## Summary
A UC ANR California Agriculture extension synthesis of the cardinal (minimum / optimum / maximum) soil temperatures governing citrus root growth. It gives the practical temperature targets a container grower actually needs — the temperatures at which roots grow best, slow down, and stop — distilled from the underlying physiology research.

## Key Findings
- **Optimum root-growth soil temperature ≈ 26°C (≈79°F)**; most active root growth between **25–30°C (77–86°F)**.
- **Minimum for root elongation ≈ 12°C (≈54°F)** — below this, root growth effectively stops.
- Root growth occurs across roughly **12–35°C (≈54–95°F)**; it falls off sharply at both ends, with sustained high root-zone temperatures driving toward the death thresholds documented in the 1985 supraoptimal/critical-temperature studies.
- Species/rootstock variation: vigorous types (e.g., rough lemon) keep growing at warmer root temperatures than slower ones (e.g., sour orange) — rootstock choice shifts the usable root-zone temperature window.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Core reference numbers for this project. A west-facing 14" pot in LA summer swings far outside the 25–30°C optimum — surface and sun-side media can exceed 35°C while shaded mornings sit below the 12°C growth minimum in winter. Keeping the root zone inside 12–30°C (shading the pot, double-potting, mulching, watering at the hot hours) is what these targets translate to operationally. Read alongside the Bevington & Castle phenology study and the container-color and mulch/insulation studies in the heat-stress topic.
