## Summary
An NC State substrates-lab study quantifying how pine-bark **source and especially age** change the physical properties that matter in a pot. Because aged pine bark is the backbone of most good citrus container mixes, this is the study that explains why "the same bark mix" can drain well when fresh and go soggy a year later.

## Key Findings
- **Aging breaks particles down into fines.** Aging bark from ≤1 to ≥6 months shifted material from the coarse (>2 mm) to the fine (<0.5 mm) fraction, **increasing container capacity by 21.4% and decreasing air space by 17.2%**.
- Finished pine bark typically has largest particles of **1/2–5/8 inch** with **~20–30% fines (<0.5 mm)**; those fines create the micropores that hold water — too many and the mix waterlogs.
- Best-management container-capacity range for pine-bark substrate is **45–65%**; total porosity for differently screened barks ran **~78–81%**.
- Aged bark is favored for being stable, low in weed seed, and slow to decompose — but its drainage advantage erodes as it continues to break down in the pot.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant and practical. It means the Lisbon's bark mix will *get wetter and less aerated over time* as bark fines accumulate — a compounding risk in a shallow 14" pot that already holds a deep perched water zone. Start with screened, coarse, partially-aged bark (limit <0.5 mm fines), and treat bark breakdown as a trigger for media refresh. Connects directly to the substrate-degradation lifecycle study and the air/water-porosity targets.
