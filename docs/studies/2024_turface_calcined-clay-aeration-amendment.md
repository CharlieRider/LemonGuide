## Summary
Technical reference on calcined montmorillonite/kaolin clay (Turface and similar) as a hard-fired, porous mineral aggregate used to add aeration and internal moisture buffering to container mixes — the key alternative to perlite/pumice for growers wanting a non-floating, non-degrading aggregate (the basis of "gritty mix").

## Key Findings
- Calcined clay is fired at high temperature into hard, porous granules with **~74% internal porosity** that **absorb roughly their own weight in water** while remaining structurally stable.
- A representative calcined-clay conditioner partitions as **~26% solids / 39% water-holding / 35% air-holding** — i.e., it adds aeration *and* a moisture reservoir simultaneously, unlike perlite (mostly air) or fine peat (mostly water).
- **Does not compact, float, or break down** over years — it holds its structure where perlite crushes and bark decomposes, making it a "permanent" aeration component.
- Largely pH-neutral and low-dust when pre-screened; the internal pores buffer both water and nutrients (modest CEC) between irrigations.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Useful as a durable aggregate for a long-lived (10-year) container: calcined clay won't crush or decompose like perlite/bark, so a mix built with it resists the "gets soggier every year" drift documented in the pine-bark-aging study. Its internal water buffer also softens the fast dry-down of a shallow west-facing pot. Tradeoff: it is heavy (a concern for a balcony) and is a commercial technical source, not peer-reviewed — treat the porosity figures as manufacturer specs. Pair with pumice-vs-perlite and air/water-porosity studies.
