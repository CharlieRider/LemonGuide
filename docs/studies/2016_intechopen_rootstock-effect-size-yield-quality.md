## Summary
A review of *how much* rootstock changes the scion — quantifying the spread in tree size, yield efficiency, and fruit quality across rootstocks, and the physiology (photosynthesis, carbohydrate partitioning) behind dwarfing. This is the "how big a difference does rootstock make" study that justifies caring about the choice at all.

## Key Findings
- **Tree size spans a huge range**: Alemow induced canopies up to **~60% larger** than sour orange; **Flying Dragon dwarfed canopy volume by ~80%** — same scion, radically different tree.
- **Yield efficiency** (fruit per canopy volume) differed sharply: **Carrizo ~9.7 and Swingle ~9.5 kg/m³** (≈30% above sour orange) vs. **Alemow ~1.8 kg/m³** — vigorous rootstocks make wood, not proportionally more fruit.
- **Dwarfing rootstocks (e.g., Flying Dragon, Forner-Alcaide selections) reduce size without necessarily cutting yield efficiency** and can improve alternate-bearing — small tree, still productive.
- Dwarfing acts through altered **photosynthate partitioning and graft-union hydraulics**, not mere starvation.
- Rootstock also shifts fruit quality (TSS, acidity, size) and maturity timing.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
This is the quantitative case for putting the Lisbon on a **dwarfing rootstock**: Flying Dragon can cut tree size ~80% while keeping fruit-per-volume efficiency high — exactly what a 14" pot needs (small, manageable, still bears). Conversely a vigorous lemon rootstock (Alemow/Volkamer) makes a big tree with *low* yield efficiency — the worst combination for a container. Ties together the Flying Dragon, C-35, Alemow, Volkamer, and trifoliate-dwarfing-mechanism studies.
