## Summary
A controlled UF/IFAS trial comparing three container types — chemical (copper-coated) pruning containers (CPCs), air-pruning containers (APCs), and standard nonpruning containers (NPCs) — on the root architecture and growth of nursery citrus. It quantifies how root pruning at the container wall changes the root system, the single most relevant Topic-1 question for whether to grow the Lisbon in a fabric/air-pruning pot vs. a solid pot.

## Key Findings
- Both CPCs and APCs produced **fibrous root pruning at the soil–container-wall interface**; NPCs did not — standard solid pots let roots reach the wall and circle.
- CPCs produced **finer roots with higher specific root length** (CPC 25.4 m/g vs. APC 21.6 m/g) — i.e., more feeder-root surface per gram.
- CPCs had a **higher root respiration rate** (1.06 mg CO₂·g⁻¹·h⁻¹) than NPCs (0.74) — denser metabolically active fibrous roots.
- Grafted trees (15 months): CPCs tallest at **114 cm** vs. NPC **96 cm**; rootstock trunk diameter **13.1 mm (CPC)** vs. **11.9 mm (NPC)**; nonfibrous root biomass **18.0 g (CPC)** vs. **13.3 g (NPC)**.
- **Fibrous root biomass did not differ significantly** among container types in grafted trees — the benefit of air/chemical pruning is root *distribution and quality* (less circling, finer roots), not necessarily more total fibrous mass.
- Nongrafted plants (3 months): CPC shoot biomass 27.3 g vs. APC 17.6 g; trunk diameter unaffected (5.3–5.4 mm).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly relevant. In a shallow 14" pot, root circling against the wall is a real long-term failure mode, and this study confirms air-pruning (fabric pot) eliminates wall-circling and shifts the tree toward finer feeder roots. The catch: APCs gave somewhat *less* shoot biomass than copper-pruning in the nursery phase, and a fabric pot dries faster — a tradeoff against the west-facing LA heat load. Pair with the air-pruning fabric-pot note already in the repo.
