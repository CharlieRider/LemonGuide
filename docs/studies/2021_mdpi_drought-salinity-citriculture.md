## Summary
Review of citrus responses to drought and salinity and how to mitigate them. Important because citrus is among the more salt-sensitive tree crops, and a leaching container irrigated with hard water concentrates exactly the salts (Cl⁻, Na⁺) that injure it.

## Key Findings
- Citrus is **salt-sensitive**: the classic threshold is **soil saturation-extract EC (ECe) ≈ 1.4 dS/m**, with yield declining ~**13% per additional dS/m** above threshold (Maas–Hoffman framework cited in citrus literature).
- **Chloride and sodium accumulate in leaves and cause marginal/tip leaf burn**; rootstock strongly modulates Cl⁻ exclusion (Topic 5 link).
- **Water stress timing matters**: stress during flowering raises flower/fruit drop; stress during rapid fruit growth reduces fruit size.
- Mitigation: maintain adequate **leaching to keep root-zone salts below threshold**, use good-quality water, avoid letting the root zone swing to extreme dryness.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant. The salt-sensitivity threshold (ECe ~1.4 dS/m) sets why the Topic-4 leaching-fraction discipline matters: a 14" pot watered with alkaline LA tap water will creep toward injurious EC without routine flushing. Also flags that drought stress at bloom/fruit-set directly costs yield — so the small reservoir must not be allowed to fully dry during those stages.
