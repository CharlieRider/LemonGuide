## Summary
A plant-physiology modelling study of *why* roots stop elongating in waterlogged, low-oxygen soil. It shows that the depth a root can grow into an anoxic zone is set jointly by how fast the root tissue consumes oxygen and how easily oxygen diffuses down the root — explaining mechanistically what happens in the saturated perched layer at the bottom of a pot.

## Key Findings
- In flooded soil, oxygen reaches the root tip only by diffusing internally from the aerated top; predicted **maximum root length rose with cortical porosity**: ~107 mm at 10% porosity, ~210 mm at 20%, ~293 mm at 30%, ~343 mm at 50% (diminishing returns past ~30%).
- **Root oxygen consumption is the more powerful lever**: halving respiration in a 20%-porosity root raised predicted reach from 210 mm to >350 mm, while doubling consumption cut it to ~110 mm.
- Oxygen demand is concentrated at the tip — apical respiration 3.8 nmol O₂·g⁻¹·s⁻¹ vs. 1.5 basal — so the growing front is the first thing starved when the surrounding medium goes anoxic.
- In thick roots (≈1900 µm), internal pO₂ fell from 20.6 kPa at the surface to **0.3 kPa** at the core — large-diameter roots self-asphyxiate fastest in saturated zones.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Extrapolated (not a citrus study) but mechanistically on point. It explains *why* citrus roots will not penetrate the chronically saturated perched layer at the base of a shallow pot: that band is functionally anoxic and a root can only extend a finite distance into it before its tip suffocates. The practical lesson reinforces the rest of Topic 1 — minimise the saturated zone with a coarse mix and adequate pot height rather than expecting roots to "tough it out."
