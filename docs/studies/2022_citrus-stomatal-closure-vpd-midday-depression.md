## Summary
Explains the physiology behind why citrus "shuts down" in hot, dry afternoons: as an **isohydric** plant, citrus closes stomata under high vapor-pressure deficit (VPD) to conserve water, causing a **midday depression** of photosynthesis. Key to understanding why a west-facing tree can look stressed at midday even when well-watered.

## Key Findings
- Citrus is **isohydric**: high VPD (hot, dry air) triggers **stomatal closure even when soil moisture is adequate** — the tree prioritizes avoiding dehydration over carbon gain.
- This produces a **midday depression**: stomata open in morning light but **close in the high-VPD heat of midday**, so photosynthesis and transpiration dip in the middle of hot, sunny days.
- Rising **air temperature raises VPD**, increasing stomatal limitation — heat reduces growth partly *through* this water-conserving stomatal closure, not only through direct tissue damage.
- Reduced midday transpiration is a **water-conservation trait**, but it also caps CO₂ uptake and growth on extreme days.
- Implication: on hot afternoons the limit is often **atmospheric (VPD), not soil water** — adding more water won't reopen the stomata.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Clarifies a common misread for the Lisbon: midday leaf wilting/curling and apparent "thirst" on a hot west-facing balcony is frequently **VPD-driven stomatal closure, not drought** — so reflexively dumping more water (in an already moisture-limited shallow pot) doesn't help and can waterlog roots. The real levers are **lowering VPD/heat load** — afternoon shade, evaporative cooling, raising local humidity — rather than over-watering. Distinguish true wilting (dry pot, by weight) from heat-VPD closure. Pairs with the watering, marine-layer, shade-cloth, and evaporative-cooling studies.
