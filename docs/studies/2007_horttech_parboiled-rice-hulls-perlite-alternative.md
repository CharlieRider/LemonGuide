## Summary
Peer-reviewed comparison of **parboiled fresh rice hulls (PBH) vs. perlite** as the aeration aggregate in peat-based mixes. Establishes that rice hulls — a renewable, low-dust, lightweight byproduct — can replace perlite for aeration with broadly similar physical properties.

## Key Findings
- Ground/screened PBH had **total pore space, air-filled pore space, and water-holding capacity similar to sphagnum peat**.
- Substituting **20–40% PBH for perlite** gave substrates with **similar air-filled porosity** but somewhat **lower total pore space and water-holding capacity** than the equivalent perlite mixes — i.e., PBH aerates comparably but holds a bit less water.
- PBH is **lightweight (~1 lb/gal)**, low-dust, near-neutral pH (little effect on substrate pH), and free of viable weed/rice seed after parboiling.
- Practical aeration loading is around **30%** of mix volume; the main drawback is that PBH **decomposes over time** (unlike perlite/pumice/calcined clay), so aeration declines across a long pot life.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A renewable, lightweight aeration option attractive for a balcony pot where weight matters — PBH gives perlite-like air space without perlite's dust or floating. The catch for a 10-year container: rice hulls break down within a year or two, so for a long-lived Lisbon they suit a mix you refresh regularly rather than a "permanent" aggregate like pumice or calcined clay. Compare with the pumice-vs-perlite and calcined-clay studies.
