## Summary
Reviews **girdling (scoring)** — a shallow bark incision that temporarily blocks downward carbohydrate flow, diverting sugars to flowers/fruit — and related cultural practices for boosting fruit set. A high-impact but high-skill technique relevant to coaxing more fruit from a small tree.

## Key Findings
- Girdling at **petal fall / end of bloom** markedly improves fruit set by **raising carbohydrate availability** to the developing fruit (it works even in parthenocarpic cultivars, since the limit is carbohydrate, not pollination).
- Reported yield increases are large: **+73% to +400%** from trunk/branch girdling timed to fruit set.
- **Summer girdling increases fruit size** and improves internal quality (higher sugars, better color/firmness).
- **Risk**: girdling demands precision — cut too deep or leave it unhealed and you can **permanently damage or kill** the limb/tree; the girdle must heal over.
- Other set-boosting cultural levers: pollinator access, balanced nutrition, and avoiding stress at bloom/set.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A powerful but **advanced, optional** tool for the Lisbon — a careful branch girdle at petal fall could meaningfully raise set/yield on a small tree, and summer girdling could size up fruit. But on a single irreplaceable container specimen the **downside risk (killing a scaffold) is serious**, so it's a technique for an experienced grower, not a default. Safer first levers for a balcony tree are pollinator access, steady carbohydrate supply (light, nutrition), and not stressing the tree at bloom. Connects to the parthenocarpy, alternate-bearing, pruning, and nutrition studies.
