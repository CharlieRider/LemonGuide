## Summary
The bonsai toolkit for holding citrus very small and dense in a container — clip-and-grow shaping, ramification (branching multiplication), and pinching. The extreme end of size-control pruning, useful as a reference for anyone wanting to keep a Lisbon deliberately compact in a 14" pot rather than merely "managed."

## Key Findings
- **Ramification** (turning one branch into many fine secondary branches) is built by **repeatedly pinching/cutting back new shoots** — citrus shoots are cut back to **~2 leaves after 4–6 leaves/nodes** have extended.
- **"The finer the branch, the finer the leaf"** — ramification naturally **reduces leaf size**, keeping the tree in scale with a small pot.
- **Clip-and-grow** (selective tip pruning to redirect growth) shapes structure without wiring; fine wire can assist styling.
- **Defoliation** forces a flush of new, smaller leaves and increases ramification — an advanced technique that must be used sparingly on fruiting citrus.
- A **pot-bound / restricted root mass naturally curbs leaf size and vigor** — root confinement is part of the miniaturizing system (consistent with the root-restriction findings in Topic 1).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Optional but directly on-point: even without going full bonsai, the **clip-and-grow + pinch-after-4–6-nodes** approach is an effective way to hold a vigorous Lisbon compact and densely twiggy in a 14" pot, and the root-restriction-shrinks-the-top principle explains why the pot itself helps. The trade-off is fruit: heavy pinching/defoliation favors miniaturization over crop, so a fruiting balcony tree uses a lighter version of these techniques. Connects to the size-control, root-pruning, root-restriction (Topic 1), and maintain-at-fixed-size (lifecycle) studies.
