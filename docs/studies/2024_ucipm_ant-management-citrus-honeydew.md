## Summary
UC IPM guidance on managing ants — chiefly the Argentine ant, ubiquitous in coastal Southern California — because ants are the hidden driver of soft-scale, mealybug, aphid, and whitefly outbreaks. Controlling ants is often the single highest-leverage IPM action on a citrus tree.

## Key Findings
- Ants **feed on honeydew** excreted by soft scales, mealybugs, cottony cushion scale, whiteflies, psyllids, and aphids — and in return **protect those pests from their natural enemies**, breaking biological control.
- So an ant problem usually means (or soon causes) a **scale/mealybug/aphid + sooty-mold** problem; cut off the ants and natural enemies often clean up the honeydew pests on their own.
- **Trunk barriers** — sticky bands (e.g., Tanglefoot) on the trunk, combined with **skirt-pruning** to remove other access routes — physically block ants from the canopy.
- **Baits are the preferred chemical control** (ants carry slow-acting bait back to the colony); **boric acid liquid bait stations** are an organic option, refilled often.
- Monitor in spring when honeydew pests appear; ants with swollen translucent abdomens are honeydew collectors.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A top-priority, easy win for the Lisbon: an isolated balcony pot is **trivial to ant-proof** — a sticky band around the trunk or pot, kept clear of leaf/wall bridges (skirt the canopy), severs the ant–honeydew-pest partnership and lets the existing scale/aphid/mealybug/whitefly guidance (and natural enemies) work. If sooty mold or scale appears, **check for and stop the ants first**. Ties directly to the scale/mealybug/sooty-mold, aphid, whitefly, and skirt-pruning studies.
