## Summary
Why over-pruning is a real risk for container citrus and how the root-to-canopy balance governs container health. Counterweight to the temptation to hard-prune a vigorous Lisbon.

## Key Findings
- **Maintaining proportional balance between canopy and roots is fundamental** to container success — neither should be drastically reduced without the other.
- **Over-pruning the canopy** removes the leaf area that feeds roots and fruit, **provoking vigorous, thorny regrowth** and reducing the crop (citrus fruits on a substantial leaf base; <2% of flowers become fruit, so leaf area per fruit matters).
- **Citrus does not need annual heavy pruning** the way deciduous fruit does — light, regular shaping is sufficient.
- Removing too much canopy also **exposes bark to sunburn**, especially severe on a west-facing balcony.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Important guardrail. With Lisbon's vigor, the instinct is to cut hard — but over-pruning triggers a thorny water-sprout flush, costs the crop, and risks sunburn. The discipline is **light frequent shaping + matched root pruning every 2–3 years**, keeping top and roots in proportion, rather than periodic severe cutbacks. Restraint is the rule for this shallow-pot, hot-exposure tree.
