## Summary
An IFAS extension piece on *why* selective pruning improves fruit, not just tree shape: it gets light into the canopy interior, which drives flowering and fruit quality. Connects pruning directly to the sugar content and appearance of the fruit a container grower actually harvests.

## Key Findings
- **The biggest long-term benefit is light penetration deep into the canopy** — cuts (especially removing watersprouts and crowded interior branches) **open the center, increasing flowering and interior fruiting**.
- **Shaded interior fruit have lower soluble solids (sugar)** than exterior fruit, because fruit draw sugars mainly from their **nearest leaves** — a dark canopy core makes poor fruit.
- Selective pruning improves **fruit set and quality**, including **peel color, texture, and fruit shape**.
- Removing dead wood eliminates spore reservoirs, **reducing fruit blemishes and postharvest decay** and improving shelf life.
- Proactive selective pruning keeps the canopy a **manageable size while improving both yield and quality**.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Reframes pruning the Lisbon as a fruit-quality tool, not just size control: a small tree can still develop a dense, shaded core, so **open the interior with selective thinning cuts** to light up inner wood — that lifts flowering, fruit set, sugar/peel quality, and reduces disease. For a west-facing balcony tree, balance this against bark-sunburn risk (don't strip so much that the trunk/scaffolds are fully exposed — see sunburn study). Pairs with the Mediterranean-pruning, structural-pruning, and fruiting studies.
