## Summary
Extension overview of how to *measure* root-zone moisture rather than water on a fixed schedule — the missing feedback tool for a container grower trying to avoid both drought and waterlogging in a pot whose moisture swings fast.

## Key Findings
- Two things are measured: **moisture content** (how much water is present) and **moisture tension** (how tightly it's held) — tension is what the plant "feels."
- **Tensiometers** read soil water tension via a water-filled tube with a ceramic tip; as the mix dries, suction builds and the gauge rises — common in frequently-irrigated, closely-monitored horticulture (i.e., containers).
- **Electrical-resistance (gypsum) blocks** estimate tension cheaply via resistance between embedded electrodes; **capacitance/TDR sensors** read volumetric content electronically.
- **Gravimetric (weight) method** — weigh the pot wet vs. dry — is the calibration reference and, for a single container, the simplest reliable gauge: a pot noticeably light = time to water.
- Readings convert (via charts) to a soil-water deficit that triggers irrigation, replacing calendar watering.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly actionable and cheap for one pot. The **lift-the-pot / weight method** is the most practical single-container technique — learn the saturated vs. "needs water" weight and water by feel of heft. A small tensiometer or capacitance probe in the root zone adds precision and is ideal where the goal is to never let a 14" west-facing pot either bake bone-dry (hard to rewet — see hydration study) or sit saturated (root rot). Pairs with the overwatering, under/over-diagnosis, and water-stress-ID studies.
