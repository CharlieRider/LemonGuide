## Summary
Covers **skirting** — removing the lowest canopy branches to lift the foliage off the ground — as a disease-management pruning practice. It reduces soil-splash infection and improves airflow under the tree, both directly relevant to a shallow, sometimes-wet container in a humid marine-layer climate.

## Key Findings
- **Phytophthora brown-rot** spores live in the soil/media and are **splashed up onto low-hanging fruit** by rain/irrigation; skirting removes the low fruit from the splash zone.
- Removing branches within **~30 inches (≈75 cm)** of the soil significantly cuts fruit decay; trials (1987–89) found **brown-rot incidence within 48 in. of the ground was significantly higher in non-skirted than skirted** trees.
- Skirting **improves airflow and lowers humidity** under the canopy and around the trunk, reducing persistence of moisture that favors fungal/oomycete disease.
- It also keeps foliage out of contact with the ground (pest highways, soil organisms).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Adapted to scale, the principle is valuable for the Lisbon: keep the lowest branches and any fruit **clear of the pot surface and mulch** so irrigation splash from the (occasionally Phytophthora-harboring) media doesn't reach fruit and low leaves, and so the marine-layer humidity under the canopy can dry. On a balcony, lifting the skirt a few inches above the rim improves airflow and reduces brown-rot and soil-splash risk. Connects strongly to the Phytophthora, greasy-spot, marine-layer, and overwatering studies.
