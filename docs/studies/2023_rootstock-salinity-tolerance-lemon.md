## Summary
Synthesis of how rootstock governs a lemon tree's salt tolerance — the trait that matters most for a container irrigated with alkaline, chloride-bearing municipal water. Citrus salt injury is largely a **chloride/sodium-exclusion** problem, and the rootstock root system is the gatekeeper.

## Key Findings
- Salt injury in citrus comes mainly from **Cl⁻ and Na⁺ accumulating in leaves**; rootstocks differ chiefly in how well their roots **exclude/retard** these ions.
- **Salt-tolerance ranking (roots that exclude Cl/Na best → worst)**: **Cleopatra mandarin and Citrus macrophylla (Alemow) ≈ most tolerant > Volkameriana (moderate) > Swingle citrumelo > Carrizo/Troyer citrange (sensitive — accumulate Cl rapidly) ≈ trifoliate (sensitive)**.
- Alemow threshold ≈ **1.02 dS/m**, then ~**14.2% yield loss per dS/m** above it — even "tolerant" rootstocks have limits.
- The cruel trade-off: the **most salt-tolerant lemon rootstocks (Alemow, Volkamer) are the most vigorous and least size-controlling**, while the **size-controlling trifoliate types are the most salt-sensitive** — you usually can't get both.
- Mitigation regardless of rootstock: **leach the root zone** (20%+ leaching fraction) to keep Cl/Na from concentrating.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
The crux decision for a coastal-LA Lisbon: if tap-water chloride is high, a salt-tolerant rootstock (Alemow/Volkamer) protects the tree — but costs size control, forcing heavy pruning in a 14" pot. The usual better path is a **size-controlling trifoliate rootstock plus disciplined salt management** (low-Cl water or rainwater, generous leaching, periodic flushes) rather than a vigorous salt-tolerant rootstock. Directly ties the rootstock choice to the irrigation-water-quality, leaching-fraction, and EC/soluble-salts studies.
