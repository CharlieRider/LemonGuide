## Summary
Extension guidance on measuring and managing soluble-salt (EC) buildup in container media — the slow-motion nutrition failure where fertilizer salts accumulate faster than they leach and start burning roots. Highly relevant because a confined pot concentrates salts that an in-ground tree would dilute.

## Key Findings
- **EC above ~2.0 mmho/cm (dS/m)** in the root zone is generally detrimental; sustained high salts cause root injury, leaf-tip/margin burn, chlorosis, and wilting.
- Salt sources stack up in a pot: water-soluble and controlled-release fertilizers, irrigation-water salts, residual fertilizer, and salts released by organic-matter breakdown.
- **Leach to control salts**: apply enough water for a **10–30% leaching fraction** (≈20% extra beyond container capacity) at each or periodic irrigations to flush accumulated salts out the bottom.
- "Salt index" ranks fertilizers by how much EC they add per unit weight (sodium nitrate = 100); high-salt-index materials raise root-zone EC fastest, so choose lower-salt-index or controlled-release forms.
- Correct high EC by **reducing fertilizer concentration/frequency and leaching thoroughly**.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Central risk for this setup: alkaline LA tap water plus year-round feeding in a small pot drives EC up, and a shallow 14" container with reduced reservoir leaches less freely. Manage by feeding dilute-and-often (EC < ~1.5–2.0 dS/m), choosing low-salt-index/controlled-release fertilizer, and **periodically flushing with a 20%+ leaching fraction** — especially before winter when uptake slows. Directly ties to the leaching-fraction (SPES-128), CRF/N-leaching, and long-term salt-accumulation lifecycle studies.
