## Summary
Explains the two kinds of unwanted vigorous growth — **watersprouts** (vertical shoots from trunk/branches, the desired scion) and **rootstock suckers** (growth from below the graft, the wrong variety) — and why the latter must be removed promptly. A critical maintenance task on any grafted citrus, easy to neglect on a container tree.

## Key Findings
- **Rootstock suckers** arise **below the graft union** or from roots; they are the **rootstock variety, not the Lisbon** (often bitter trifoliate/sour-orange type) and will bear inedible fruit.
- Rootstock is more vigorous than the scion, so **unchecked suckers can outgrow and crowd out the desirable top**, eventually "taking over" the tree.
- **Remove suckers while young**: grasp and pull downward so they tear off cleanly at the base (more complete removal than a cut, which can regrow); cut close to the source if too large; dig down to sever root suckers.
- **Watersprouts** (above the graft) are real scion wood — manage selectively (some can become fruiting wood; remove the rest to open the canopy).
- A **white cardboard/wrap collar around the lower 10–12 in. of trunk** discourages low sucker growth; don't paint sucker wounds.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Essential ongoing care for the Lisbon: inspect the trunk base and soil line regularly and **rub/pull off rootstock suckers below the graft as soon as they appear** — on a small container tree a vigorous trifoliate sucker can dominate fast, and its growth wastes the scion's limited resources. Know exactly where the graft union sits so you can tell scion watersprouts (keep/manage) from rootstock suckers (always remove). Ties to the rootstock-variety and structural-pruning studies.
