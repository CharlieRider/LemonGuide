## Summary
Lays out the developmental timeline from flower to ripe lemon — the phases (set → cell division → cell expansion → maturation/color) and how long each takes. Useful for knowing when fruit needs the most water/nutrients and when to expect harvest from a given bloom flush.

## Key Findings
- **Bloom to harvest ≈ 6–9 months** for true lemons (can stretch to 5–13 months depending on cultivar, climate, and bloom timing).
- Early phase = **cell division** (fruitlet, weeks after set), then a long **cell-expansion/rapid-growth phase (~4–6 months)** where cells take up water and swell — the period of **highest water and nutrient demand**.
- Rind **starts green and turns yellow** as the fruit reaches ~3–4 inches; full color/maturity comes last.
- Because lemons everbloom, a tree can carry **fruit at several developmental stages simultaneously** (and bloom again while ripening earlier fruit).
- Grafted trees begin bearing in **~2–3 years**; seedlings 5–6 years.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Helps schedule care for the Lisbon: the long cell-expansion phase is when consistent watering and feeding matter most (water stress here causes drop or small fruit — see RDI and fruit-drop studies), and the 6–9-month timeline means a spring bloom ripens late the same year / into the next. The everbearing, multi-stage habit means the container tree will usually have some fruit developing year-round, so care can't be "switched off" seasonally the way a single-harvest crop allows. Pairs with the bloom-flush, watering, and yield-expectations studies.
