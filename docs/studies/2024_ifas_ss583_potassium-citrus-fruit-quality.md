## Summary
Synthesis of potassium's role in citrus, the nutrient that most directly governs **fruit size, rind quality, and yield**. K is applied at roughly the same rate as nitrogen, so it deserves its own entry alongside the N-rate study.

## Key Findings
- **Fruit size and rind**: K-deficient trees set **small fruit with thin rind, poor color, and more splitting/creasing**; adequate-to-high K increases fruit size (very high K gives large fruit with thick, coarse peel) — so K is dialed to a target, not maximized.
- Yield response in sweet orange trials was driven mainly by **increased fruit size** (and partly improved fruit set); a rate around **75 kg K₂O/ha** was effective for raising fruit weight/size.
- **Leaf K interpretation**: keep leaf K **above ~0.7%** to avoid deficiency; optimum sufficiency is roughly **1.2–1.7%**. Citrus tolerates a wide K range before visible symptoms appear, so leaf testing is the reliable guide.
- K is central to photosynthesis and water relations — photosynthetic rate drops sharply under K deficiency.
- IFAS sets the **K₂O application rate ≈ the N rate**, increased ~25% if leaf K runs low.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
For a Lisbon grown for fruit, K is the lever for decent-sized lemons rather than small thin-rinded ones — feed K₂O at roughly the N rate (per the SS675 young-tree figures) and verify with leaf analysis (target ~1.2–1.7%). In a fast-leaching pot K washes out alongside N, reinforcing the dilute-and-often / controlled-release approach. Connects to the N-rate, leaf-tissue-testing, and fruiting-topic studies.
