## Summary
Covers the often-skipped mechanics of *how to cut* so wounds heal and disease doesn't enter — tool sharpness, sanitation between plants, and whether to seal cuts. Important because every pruning cut on a citrus is a potential infection court, and citrus has thin, vulnerable bark.

## Key Findings
- **Clean, sharp tools make clean cuts that seal up to ~40% faster** than ragged/torn cuts; dull tools crush tissue and create slow-healing entry points for pathogens and the citrus borer.
- The tree seals small-diameter cuts itself by forming a **healing periderm/callus**; the **larger the wound, the slower and harder** it closes — favor many small cuts over a few large ones.
- **Disinfect tools between plants** (and between cuts when active disease is present) with **70% isopropyl alcohol or 10% bleach** — citrus viroids (e.g., exocortis) and vascular pathogens spread on contaminated blades.
- **Do not apply disinfectant to the wound itself** — it injures tissue and slows callus; wound dressings/wax are only worth it on large-diameter cuts.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly practical for the Lisbon: use sharp, clean bypass pruners, make small cuts the tree can seal quickly, and wipe blades with alcohol before/after working the tree (cheap insurance against importing viroids on tools used elsewhere). Skip wound paint on the small cuts a container tree needs; reserve sealing for any rare large cut. Pairs with the pruning-timing, structural-pruning, and pest-disease studies.
