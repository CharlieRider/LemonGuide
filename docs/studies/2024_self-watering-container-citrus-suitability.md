## Summary
Evaluates whether self-watering (sub-irrigated planter, SIP) pots — which wick water up from a bottom reservoir — are appropriate for citrus. The short answer is "with caution," and the reasons are specific to citrus's salt sensitivity and dislike of constant saturation.

## Key Findings
- **Salt trap (the big one)**: in a SIP, water wicks **upward** and evaporates from the surface, so **fertilizer and tap-water salts accumulate in the mix and are never flushed out the bottom** — exactly the failure mode salt-sensitive citrus is most prone to (see water-quality study).
- **Waterlogging risk**: an over-effective wick or a missing/blocked overflow keeps the lower medium **perpetually saturated**, driving the root hypoxia and Phytophthora citrus most needs to avoid in a shallow pot.
- Citrus prefers **moderate dry-down between waterings**, unlike the constant-moisture plants (herbs, leafy greens, tropicals) SIPs suit best.
- SIPs require a **light, coir/peat + perlite wicking mix**; a coarse bark/pumice citrus mix wicks poorly, so the two design goals conflict.
- Anecdotal citrus-in-SIP attempts report decline (yellowing→browning), consistent with the salt/saturation mechanisms above.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Cautionary: a self-watering pot is **not recommended as the primary system** for the Lisbon — it works against citrus on both salt flushing and aeration, and a shallow 14" pot already has a large saturated perched zone. If a reservoir is used at all (e.g., for short trips), it should be small, with a working overflow and periodic **top-watering flushes** to leach salts. A timed drip system with free drainage is the safer automation route. Connects to the overwatering/root-rot, water-quality/salinity, and perched-water-table studies.
