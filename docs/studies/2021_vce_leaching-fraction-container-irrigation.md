## Summary
Defines the leaching fraction (LF) — the share of applied water that drains out — and gives operational targets to control salt build-up in containers. The quantitative backbone for managing salt accumulation in a leaching-prone shallow citrus pot irrigated with hard LA water.

## Key Findings
- **Operational LF target: 0.15–0.30 (15–30%)** of applied water should drain through (Chappell et al. 2013); start at **~0.5 (50%)** while learning a crop, then tighten.
- LF must rise with irrigation-water salinity: **water EC < 1.0 dS/m → LF ≈ 0.1; EC 1.5 → LF ≈ 0.2; EC > 2.0 → LF ≈ 0.3** (Evans 2017).
- LF measured as **water leached ÷ water applied** (collect drainage from ≥3 pots on a warm sunny day).
- Adjust irrigation time with **TA = t × (LFT − LFM)**.
- Targeting 10–20% LF cut one nursery's water use 43% — leaching enough to flush salt, not so much as to waste water and nutrients.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly actionable and central to Topic 4. Because LA tap water is moderately saline/alkaline, irrigate the 14" pot to a **deliberate ~15–30% leaching fraction** (water until ~15–30% drains from the holes) to flush accumulated salt — heavier if a substrate EC test runs high. This is the practical defense against the salt accumulation that otherwise scorches container citrus.
