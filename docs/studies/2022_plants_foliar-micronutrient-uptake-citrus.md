## Summary
A citrus-specific study comparing **foliar vs. soil application** of the three micronutrients most often deficient in container citrus — boron, manganese, and zinc — on a sandy (fast-leaching, low-retention) soil that behaves much like a container mix. It quantifies when to spray the leaf vs. feed the root.

## Key Findings
- **Foliar uptake of micronutrients can exceed ~80% efficiency**, versus only **~10–20%** of soil-applied micronutrients becoming available — foliar is dramatically more efficient for these elements.
- **Mn and Zn**: foliar application produced leaf concentrations **~2× higher** than soil application — foliar is the better route for Mn and Zn.
- **Boron**: the *opposite* — soil application gave **~2× higher** leaf B than foliar, because B does not adsorb on sandy soil and moves readily to roots (but also leaches easily).
- **Double-rate** foliar B/Mn/Zn gave higher leaf concentrations than single-rate sprays.
- Best uptake under cool, humid conditions (morning/late afternoon) that prolong leaf wetness.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly applicable: a fast-draining 14" pot with alkaline LA tap water is a classic setting for Zn/Mn lockout, and this study says **foliar sprays of Zn and Mn are the high-efficiency fix** (soil chelates lock up at high pH). Boron is the exception — apply it to the medium, sparingly, since it leaches. Spray in the cool marine-layer morning hours. Complements the micronutrient-deficiency (SS423), chelate-selection (HS1208), and fertilizer-methods studies.
