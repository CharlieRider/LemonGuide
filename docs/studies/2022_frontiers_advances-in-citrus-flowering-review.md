## Summary
Authoritative peer-reviewed review of citrus floral induction, quantifying the cool-temperature and water-deficit triggers — and it explicitly references Lisbon lemon's flowering behavior. The physiological basis for understanding (and nudging) bloom in a mild coastal climate.

## Key Findings
- **Low-temperature induction**: trees at **15°C for ≥1.5 months flower; at 25°C for 7–9 months they stay vegetative** (Inoue 1988/1990). _CiFT2_ (flowering gene) expression begins **when minimum temperature drops below 15°C**.
- **Water-deficit induction**: leaf **Ψ ≈ −2 MPa for 40–60 days** induces flowering (Chica & Albrigo 2013) — the tropical/drought pathway.
- **Floral induction occurs 2–3 months before bud break**; differentiation at bud release.
- **Lisbon lemon specifically** flowers multiple times yearly and responds to *both* temperature and water-stress induction (Lovatt et al. 1988; Martínez-Fuentes et al. 2010).
- **GA₃ inhibits flowering** (e.g. 25 mg/L cut sweet-orange flowering 45–76%); high fall GA suppresses bud formation.
- Carbohydrate (starch/glucose) showed **no consistent correlation** with flowering intensity in Lisbon lemon.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant. Brentwood's cool, moist winters (minima often below 15°C) supply the temperature induction that drives Lisbon's heavy spring bloom — so no artificial stressing is needed. The dual induction pathways explain Lisbon's multiple flushes in this climate. Avoid GA-containing products if maximizing bloom; the temperature pathway is already favorable on the coast.
