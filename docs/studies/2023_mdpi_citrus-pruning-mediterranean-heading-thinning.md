## Summary
A review of citrus pruning specifically under **Mediterranean climate** conditions — the same climate type as coastal LA — covering cut types, how pruning redistributes vigor, timing, and effects on yield/quality. The most climate-matched general pruning reference in this topic.

## Key Findings
- **Two fundamental cut types**: **thinning cuts** remove a whole shoot/branch back to its origin → open the canopy and admit light **without stimulating dense regrowth**; **heading cuts** shorten a shoot → trigger **vigorous, bushy regrowth** of the buds just below the cut.
- Pruning is **dwarfing for the whole tree** (it removes stored carbohydrate and leaf area) but **locally invigorating** at the cut — so over-heading produces a thicket of watersprouts and less fruit.
- **Light, regular pruning beats infrequent heavy pruning**: severe pruning shifts the tree toward vegetative regrowth and **reduces the next crop**; moderate selective thinning maintains fruiting.
- **Timing** in Mediterranean climates is **after harvest / before or around the spring flush**, avoiding frost windows and the heat of mid-summer (to limit bark sunburn on newly exposed wood).
- Pruning balances canopy size, light distribution, airflow, and fruiting wood renewal.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
The governing principles for the Lisbon: favor **thinning cuts** to open and shape the canopy (light, airflow) and **minimize heading**, which on a vigorous Lisbon just spawns watersprout thickets and delays fruiting. Prune **lightly and often** rather than hard once — heavy cuts push the small tree into vegetative mode and cost fruit, and expose bark to the west sun (see sunburn study). Time work to the cool post-harvest/early-spring window. Anchors the structural-pruning, over-pruning, fruit-wood-timing, and sunburn studies.
