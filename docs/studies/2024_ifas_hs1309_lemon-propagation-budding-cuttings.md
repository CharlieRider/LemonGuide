## Summary
Covers how lemons are propagated — **budding/grafting onto a rootstock vs. rooted cuttings** — and the trade-offs for a container grower who might want to propagate a Lisbon or understand what they're buying.

## Key Findings
- **Budding (T-bud / chip bud) onto a rootstock is the standard method**: effective, needs less skill than other grafts. **T-budding requires the bark to be "slipping"** (active growth — late spring/summer in California); when bark isn't slipping, **chip budding** works instead.
- **Grafting to a rootstock yields a superior tree**: disease resistance, size control, and adaptation come from the rootstock (a Lisbon on Flying Dragon is small; on Volkamer is huge — see rootstock studies).
- **Rooted cuttings** (own-root, no rootstock) are easy and cheap, need proper warmth/humidity/light + rooting hormone, and make **robust, low-branching, bushy trees** — attractive for container culture but **forfeit rootstock benefits** (no dwarfing, less disease/salt tolerance, more Phytophthora risk).
- **Air layering** is another vegetative option.
- Seeds are not used for true-to-type fruiting trees (long juvenility, variability).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Explains the choice behind the Brentwood Lisbon: a **grafted (budded) tree on a dwarfing rootstock is strongly preferred** for a 14" pot — the rootstock delivers the size control and Phytophthora/salt tolerance a vigorous own-root Lisbon would lack. An **own-root rooted cutting** is a low-cost, bushy alternative but gives up dwarfing and disease/salt protection (risky on alkaline LA water and in a wet shallow pot). If propagating at home, bud onto a chosen rootstock in late spring/summer when bark slips. Connects to the rootstock-selection, buying-a-certified-tree, and Phytophthora studies.
