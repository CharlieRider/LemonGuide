## Summary
UC IPM guidance on the soil-borne disease most likely to kill a container citrus in a wet, shallow pot. Phytophthora attacks roots and the crown, and its risk is driven by exactly the over-wet conditions a 14" pot creates.

## Key Findings
- **Phytophthora citrophthora/parasitica cause winter and summer root rot, crown/trunk gummosis, and fruit brown rot**; severe cases kill the tree.
- **Risk is governed by soil moisture** — prolonged saturation and poor drainage are the primary drivers; **the disease spreads in standing water**.
- Management is integrated: **resistant rootstock + irrigation/drainage management + (if needed) fungicides** (fosetyl-Al/Aliette foliar; potassium phosphonate/mefenoxam soil).
- **Keep the graft union and crown dry and above the soil line**; avoid splashing soil onto the trunk.
- Trifoliate-lineage rootstocks (Flying Dragon, citranges, Swingle) are among the more tolerant (Topic 5 link).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly the shallow-pot threat. The 14" pot's large perched-water zone and winter-rain saturation are ideal Phytophthora conditions. The repository's layered defense: **(1) Phytophthora-tolerant dwarfing rootstock, (2) coarse fast-draining mix and elevated pot (Topics 1–2), (3) leach-but-drain irrigation (Topic 4), (4) keep the crown/graft above the mix.** Reserve phosphonate fungicides for confirmed problems.
