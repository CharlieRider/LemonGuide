## Summary
Explains the natural, expected shedding of fruitlets — why a citrus tree drops most of what it sets and keeps only a fraction. Crucial context so a container grower doesn't panic at normal drop or misread it as a problem.

## Key Findings
- Citrus naturally drops fruit in **three periods**: **post-bloom** (flowers/tiny fruitlets), **"June drop"** (~1–2 months after bloom; late spring), and **pre-harvest**.
- **June drop is self-thinning**: developing fruitlets compete for limited carbohydrates; weaker ones **abscise from carbohydrate starvation**, adjusting crop load to what the tree can finish — June drop alone is ~10% of total drop.
- Hormone signals from strong fruitlets cue continued carbohydrate export to them; weak fruitlets lose the signal and drop.
- **Abscission zones differ**: June-drop fruit separate at **abscission zone C (fruit–calyx junction)**; pre-harvest fruit drop at the calyx/"button."
- Drop is worsened by stress (heat, drought, low carbohydrate) layered on the natural process.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Sets realistic expectations for the Lisbon: a heavy bloom dropping most of its flowers/fruitlets is **normal and healthy**, not failure — a small container tree can only carry a limited crop, and self-thinning matches load to resources. Because June drop is carbohydrate-driven, avoid stacking *extra* stress at that window (let the tree not bake or dry out in late spring), and don't over-fertilize trying to "save" every fruitlet. Connects to the fruit/flower-drop abscission, alternate-bearing, and heat-stress studies.
