## Summary
Comprehensive Purdue extension reference on container substrate physical/chemical properties and components. Supplies the target numbers (CEC, pH, component pH values, bark fraction) used to design a long-lived citrus mix and explains why container height changes effective aeration.

## Key Findings
- **Container capacity and air space both depend on container height**: the taller the container, the more it drains, the greater the air space and the lower the water capacity — the physics that makes a 14"-deep pot wetter than a half-barrel.
- Target **CEC ≥ 6–15 meq/100 cc**; **substrate pH 5.4–6.6** for most crops (citrus optimum 5.5–6.5 overlaps this).
- Component pH values: **peat moss 3.0–4.0** (acidic, hydrophobic when dry, needs wetting agent), **perlite 6.5–7.5**, **pumice 7.0–7.4** (inert), **pine wood 4.5–6.0**.
- **Coir** has higher pH than peat (needs less lime) but **requires more Ca, S, Cu, Fe and less K**, immobilizes more N, and carries **EC/Na/Cl risk if not rinsed** — directly motivates buffering and a Ca amendment.
- Pine bark: low CEC, high C:N (N immobilization risk), but durable structure with far less shrinkage than peat over multi-year crops.
- **"Large container" mixes are 50–60% coarse bark** for drainage; 100% pine bark is viable for woody/nursery crops.
- Gypsum can be amended to **lower initial pH** while supplying Ca/S; lime (calcitic faster than dolomitic) raises pH.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Foundational. The 50–60% coarse-bark "large container" formula is the right backbone for a 14"-deep citrus pot, pumice/perlite are the pH-neutral aeration aggregates, and the coir caveats (buffer + add Ca, watch Na/EC) flag exactly what to manage if coir is used for water retention.
