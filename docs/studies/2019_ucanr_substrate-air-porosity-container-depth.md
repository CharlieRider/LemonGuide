## Summary
UC ANR extension article demonstrating how container **height** controls air-filled porosity (AFP) for an identical medium. The single most important Topic-1 finding for a shallow pot: the same mix that is well-aerated in a tall pot can be near-anaerobic in a short one.

## Key Findings
- For one identical 1:1 peat:vermiculite mix, AFP measured at container capacity scaled directly with container height:
  - **648 tray: 0.5% AFP**
  - **288 cell: 2.8% AFP**
  - **4-inch pot (3.25" tall): 13% AFP**
  - **6-inch pot (4.5" tall): 20% AFP**
- Established inverse rule: **as container height decreases, water-holding capacity increases and air-filled porosity falls** — taller pots drain the perched water more completely, leaving more air near the surface.
- A 14"-deep pot is taller than any container tested here, so the *same* mix would have AFP at or above the 6-inch pot's 20% — but the principle cuts both ways: a 14" pot is far shallower than a 24–30" half-barrel, so it holds proportionally more water and less air than the deep containers citrus is usually grown in.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant. This is the quantitative basis for choosing a coarse, bark/pumice-heavy mix in a 14"-deep pot: depth alone does not guarantee aeration, and a peat-rich mix that performs in a tall nursery can leave the bottom several inches of a 14" pot chronically wet. Pair with the 10–12% AFP hypoxia threshold from the root-hypoxia review.
