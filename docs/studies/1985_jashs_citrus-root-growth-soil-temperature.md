## Summary
A foundational citrus root-phenology study using Plexiglas-walled root observation chambers on young 'Valencia' orange trees. It establishes the *lower-end* temperature response of citrus root growth — the cold-side complement to the heat-death thresholds already in this repo — and shows that root growth comes in flushes governed by soil temperature, not a constant background process.

## Key Findings
- Under non-limiting soil moisture, **continuous root extension occurred from February to November**; the seasonal trend was significantly correlated with soil temperature.
- **Most intense root growth occurred when soil temperature was above 27°C (≈81°F)**, and growth was **limited below 22°C (≈72°F)** — citrus roots barely grow in cool media even when shoots look fine.
- Root growth occurred in flushes that tended to **alternate with shoot flushes** — the tree partitions to tops or roots, not both at once.
- Water stress suppressed root growth independently of temperature, confirming that both factors gate root extension.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Relevant as the cool-side bookend for managing root-zone temperature. In coastal Brentwood, winter and shaded-morning media temperatures in a 14" pot can sit below 22°C for long stretches, meaning root growth (and therefore nutrient/water uptake) effectively stalls in winter even though the canopy is evergreen — a reason to back off water and fertilizer in the cool months. Pair with the 1991 California Agriculture cardinal-temperature synthesis and the supraoptimal/critical-high-temperature death thresholds already catalogued.
