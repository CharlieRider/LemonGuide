## Summary
Explains, with stability data, why iron chelate choice must follow substrate pH — the single most useful fact for preventing the chronic iron chlorosis that plagues high-pH container citrus.

## Key Findings
- **Below pH 6.0**, all three chelates (EDTA, DTPA, EDDHA) stay fully stable (chelated-fraction ratio ≈ 1.0) — cheap EDTA suffices.
- **At pH 7.5**, stability diverges sharply: **Fe-EDDHA 1.0 (stable), Fe-DTPA ≈ 0.5 (50% degraded), Fe-EDTA ≈ 0.025 (~97.5% degraded)** (Norvell 1972; Havlin et al. 2005).
- **Ferrous Fe²⁺ oxidizes to plant-unavailable Fe³⁺ above pH ~5.3**; iron deficiency typically appears when pH > 7.4.
- Practical rule: **pH > 7 → use Fe-EDDHA**; below pH 6.5, EDTA/DTPA are adequate and cheaper.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly decision-guiding. Because alkaline LA irrigation water drives container substrate pH upward, an EDTA iron product will largely fail (97.5% degraded at pH 7.5) and waste money. The repository's standing recommendation for persistent chlorosis in this pot is **Fe-EDDHA**, plus keeping substrate pH ≤ 6.5 so cheaper chelates and native micronutrients stay available.
