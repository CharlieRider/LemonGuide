## Summary
Guidance on judging irrigation-water quality for citrus, a salt-sensitive crop. Because a container concentrates whatever the irrigation water carries, the chloride/sodium/boron thresholds here matter far more for a pot than for a deep-rooted in-ground tree that gets rain-flushed.

## Key Findings
- **Citrus is salt-sensitive**, mainly via toxic accumulation of **Cl⁻, Na⁺, and B** in leaves rather than osmotic effect alone.
- **Chloride is the key limiter**: yield declines when irrigation-water Cl exceeds **~152 mg/L (~4.3 meq/L)**; Cl toxicity in woody citrus is generally **more severe than Na toxicity**.
- **Sodium injury** reported at concentrations as low as **~115 ppm**; Na is first held in roots/trunk, then released to leaves after 3–4 years as sapwood converts to heartwood — a slow-building toxicity.
- Salts accumulate because ions ride the transpiration stream and stay behind as water evaporates — so they concentrate over time unless leached.
- **Leaching is the control**: each toxic ion (Na, Cl, B) is removed by applying water beyond crop need; the leaching depth required scales with the ion load.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
High-priority for Brentwood: LA municipal water can carry meaningful Cl/Na/bicarbonate, and a 14" pot concentrates those salts every time water evaporates. Two practical defenses — (1) **water with a 20%+ leaching fraction** to flush salts out the bottom (never use a closed self-watering reservoir that traps them), and (2) periodically flush heavily, especially before the low-uptake winter. If tap-water Cl is high, collected rainwater or filtered water reduces the long-term leaf-burn risk. Connects to the leaching-fraction, EC/soluble-salts, and self-watering studies.
