## Summary
Covers the simplest water-conservation tactic for a pot: a surface mulch / top-dressing that shades the medium and slows evaporation. Distinct from the heat-insulation angle in the heat-stress topic — here the focus is on cutting irrigation demand and stabilizing surface moisture.

## Key Findings
- A UF study found surface mulch **reduced soil-water loss to evaporation by ~33%**; other reports cite up to **~50%** reduction in container evaporation.
- Mulch works three ways: **shades the surface** (less direct evaporation), creates an **insulating air gap** (slows heating), and, for organic mulches, **improves the mix's water-holding capacity** as it breaks down.
- Container depth needed is modest: a **1–1.5 inch (2.5–4 cm)** layer is sufficient — much thinner than garden beds.
- Good materials for pots: **coarse pine bark, wood chips, or coco** (high moisture retention); a thin compost top-dress also trickles a little N-P-K with each watering.
- Bonus: mulch reduces soil splash onto lower leaves, lowering soil-borne foliar disease spread.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
High-value, low-effort for a west-facing balcony: a 1–1.5" coarse-bark top-dressing on the Lisbon's pot cuts summer evaporation by roughly a third, buffers the surface against the afternoon sun's drying, and stretches the interval between waterings — directly easing the fast dry-down of a shallow pot. Keep the mulch off the trunk to avoid collar rot. Overlaps usefully with the mulch/double-pot root-heat study (thermal) and the drip-irrigation and water-stress studies.
