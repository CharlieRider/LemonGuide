## Summary
Summarizes the disease/disorder sensitivities specific to lemons and the Lisbon cultivar — the "known weaknesses" to watch, complementing the general pest-disease topic with Lisbon-specific susceptibility.

## Key Findings
- **Mal secco** (*Plenodomus tracheiphilus*, a serious vascular fungal disease of lemons): **Lisbon is susceptible** (classed with the susceptible Femminello, unlike tolerant selections); susceptibility correlates with **higher xylem vessel density**. *Note: mal secco is a Mediterranean/Black Sea disease **not currently present in California** — relevant to global Lisbon culture and biosecurity, not a current LA threat.*
- As a **true lemon (Citrus limon)**, Lisbon is among the **most cold-tender** citrus (killed in the mid-to-high 20s°F — see freeze-threshold study) and is prone to the lemon pest/disease set: **Phytophthora** (worse on vigorous lemon rootstocks), greasy spot in humid coastal air, and the **coastal-lemon bud mite/silver mite** (see those studies).
- Like all citrus in California it is a host for **ACP/HLB** (quarantine pest) and susceptible to **CTV (tristeza)** — mitigated by certified budwood and CTV-tolerant rootstocks.
- General lemon vigor makes Lisbon relatively **tolerant of heat, wind, and neglect** — its strengths are abiotic toughness, not disease immunity.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A focused watch-list for the Brentwood Lisbon: **mal secco is irrelevant in California** (don't import lemon material from infected regions), but the practical local concerns are **Phytophthora in the wet shallow pot, greasy spot in the marine layer, coastal bud/silver mite, and the ACP/HLB quarantine**. Defenses already in the repository apply — certified tree + dwarfing/Phytophthora-tolerant rootstock, free-draining mix, airflow/skirting, and IPM. Lisbon's cold-tenderness means rare-frost protection too. Connects to the Phytophthora, greasy-spot, bud-mite, ACP/HLB, freeze-threshold, and certified-tree studies.
