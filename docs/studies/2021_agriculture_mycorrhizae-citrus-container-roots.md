## Summary
Reviews the symbiosis between citrus roots and **arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF)** — unusually important for citrus because citrus has notably sparse, short root hairs and depends on fungal partners for efficient uptake. Relevant to building durable root-zone health in a long-lived container.

## Key Findings
- **Citrus has very few, short root hairs**, so it relies heavily on **mycorrhizal colonization** to absorb water and nutrients — AMF effectively extend the root system.
- AMF markedly improve uptake of the **less-mobile nutrients — phosphorus (P), zinc (Zn), copper (Cu)** — exactly the ones prone to lockout in high-pH container conditions.
- Inoculation increased citrus **plant height, stem diameter, leaf area, and shoot/root biomass**, with benefits pronounced under **moderate water stress** (drier soils).
- AMF can **suppress Phytophthora** and improve **fruit quality and root physiological activity** (field-inoculation studies).
- Fungi also improve **soil aggregation/structure**, supporting the broader rhizosphere microbial community.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
A useful long-term root-health input for the Lisbon: a sterile bagged potting mix usually starts **without** mycorrhizae, so adding an **AMF inoculant at potting/repotting** can improve P/Zn/Cu uptake (helpful against the high-pH micronutrient lockout of alkaline LA water), bolster drought resilience in a fast-drying pot, and offer some Phytophthora suppression. Effects are strongest when not over-fertilized with soluble P (high P suppresses colonization). Connects to the micronutrient, pH-management, Phytophthora, and biochar (soil-biology) studies.
