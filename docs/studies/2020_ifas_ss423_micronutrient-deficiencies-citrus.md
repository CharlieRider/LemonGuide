## Summary
UF/IFAS reference on the three micronutrients most often deficient in citrus, with the pH-vs-chelate guidance and correction rates. The micronutrient counterpart to the UC IPM nitrogen schedule — and the deficiencies a high-pH, leaching container most often shows.

## Key Findings
- **Iron chelate effective pH ranges**: Fe-EDTA and Fe-HEDTA 4.0–6.5; **Fe-DTPA 4.0–7.5; Fe-EDDHA 4.0–9.0** — EDDHA is the only reliable Fe source above pH ~7.4.
- **Zinc correction**: foliar **2–4 lb elemental Zn/acre** (sulfate/oxide/nitrate), optionally + 2–3 lb/acre hydrated lime to reduce foliage injury; apply to ⅔–fully-expanded spring-flush leaves.
- **Manganese correction**: foliar **2–3 lb elemental Mn/acre**; adding 7–10 lb/acre low-biuret urea boosts uptake.
- **Zn precipitates at alkaline pH**; deficiency favored where pH > 6.5, and **Zn is commonly deficient in containers** where leaching strips it.
- Fe correction is best done as soil-applied chelate (not foliar) because foliar Fe is poorly translocated.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant. LA tap water is typically alkaline, pushing substrate pH up over time; combined with rapid container leaching this makes Fe/Zn/Mn deficiency likely. Practical translation: keep substrate pH ≤ 6.5, use **Fe-EDDHA** if chlorosis persists, and apply Zn/Mn as dilute spring foliar sprays (field lb/acre rates do not transfer — use a citrus micronutrient foliar product per label).
