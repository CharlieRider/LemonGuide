## Summary
A lemon-specific field study of **regulated deficit irrigation (RDI)** — deliberately withholding water during drought-tolerant growth phases to save water without losing crop. One of the few irrigation studies on *Citrus limon* itself, directly informing how much a lemon can be dried down safely.

## Key Findings
- Fino lemon tolerated **20–30% irrigation water savings** under RDI **without significant yield loss**.
- Broader citrus RDI/PRD trials report water cuts of **25% (sustained deficit), 33% (RDI), up to 49% (partial root-zone drying)** with no yield penalty when timed to non-critical phases.
- Deficit timing matters: stressing during early fruit growth mainly **delayed reaching marketable fruit size** rather than cutting total yield.
- Fruit quality often **improved**: higher total soluble solids, higher vitamin C and proline, and better storage life vs. fully-irrigated controls.
- The tree's water status (stem/leaf water potential) recovered quickly on rewatering — lemons handle controlled, timed drought well.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Reassuring for a fast-drying balcony pot: a Lisbon lemon can withstand short, controlled dry-downs between waterings without harm, and mild deficit can even sharpen fruit quality — so the goal is consistent moderate moisture, not constant saturation (which is the real danger in a shallow pot). The caveat is scale: a 14" container has a tiny reservoir, so "deficit" arrives within a day or two, not weeks — use the tree's tolerance as a safety margin, not a watering strategy. Pairs with the water-stress-ID, overwatering, and drought-watering studies.
