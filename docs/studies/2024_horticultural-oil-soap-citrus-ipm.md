## Summary
Covers the workhorse least-toxic treatments for home citrus — **horticultural (mineral) oils, neem oil, and insecticidal soaps** — that handle most soft-bodied pests (scale, mites, aphids, mealybugs, whitefly) while sparing beneficials. The default chemical layer for a container IPM program, plus the safety rules that prevent leaf burn.

## Key Findings
- Oils and soaps kill **soft-bodied pests on contact** (scale crawlers, mites, aphids, mealybugs, whitefly nymphs) with **short residual** — so they spare natural enemies and fit IPM; thorough coverage (incl. leaf undersides) is essential.
- **Phytotoxicity rule: do not spray when temperatures are near/above ~90°F** (and avoid drought-stressed plants) — heat + oil burns foliage; a "lens effect" can also discolor waxy leaves.
- Apply in **early morning or late evening** to protect bees (oils can be toxic to bees when wet) and reduce burn.
- Horticultural oil also suppresses some diseases (greasy spot, powdery mildew) and **smothers overwintering pests/eggs** as a dormant-season application.
- Neem oil is practically non-toxic to birds, mammals, and (when dry/applied off-bee-hours) bees.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
The everyday treatment toolkit for the Lisbon: most balcony-citrus pest problems (scale, spider/rust mites, aphids, mealybugs, whitefly) can be managed with **horticultural oil or insecticidal soap** rather than systemic insecticides — preserving the natural enemies and pollinators the other studies rely on. Critical caution for a **west-facing balcony**: never spray oil during the hot afternoon or in a heat wave (>90°F → leaf burn) — treat at dawn/dusk on a mild day. Ties together the scale/mealybug, spider-mite, bud/rust-mite, whitefly, aphid, and greasy-spot studies.
