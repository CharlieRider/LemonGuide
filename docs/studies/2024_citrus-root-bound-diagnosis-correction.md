## Summary
How to read and fix the root ball — the diagnostic skill behind the repotting/decline cycle. Circling, girdling roots in a long-lived container tree slowly choke water and nutrient uptake; catching and correcting them at repotting is what keeps a 10-year container tree healthy.

## Key Findings
- **Inspect by sliding the tree out of the pot**: circling or girdling roots (especially any **above the soil line** or wrapping the trunk) signal a root-bound condition; a solid mat of roots with little visible mix = overdue.
- **Roots that began circling in a pot won't straighten on their own**, but they **stop circling once cut/redirected** — so correction at repotting matters.
- **Correction methods**: gently **tease/loosen** the outer roots (encourages outward growth); the **"box cut"** (slicing off the outer circling layer on 4 sides + bottom) is the most effective fix for an established circling mat; simple shallow slicing does little for already-circling roots.
- **Removing up to ~1/3 of the root mass is safe** for a severely root-bound plant.
- Left uncorrected, girdling roots progressively reduce uptake and can strangle the trunk over years.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Core long-term skill for the Lisbon: at each repotting (and especially before any size-hold root pruning), **slide the tree out and inspect** — tease out or box-cut circling roots so they don't girdle, and remove up to a third of a badly bound mass. Because a 14" pot fills with roots relatively fast, this inspection should be routine, and it dovetails with the root-pruning-to-hold-size strategy (you're correcting circling *and* controlling size in one operation).
