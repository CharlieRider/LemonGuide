## Summary
A controlled greenhouse study amending the growing medium of four citrus rootstocks with biochar at 0/10/20/30% by volume, measuring root architecture, nutrient uptake, and soil properties. It is the most citrus-specific substrate-amendment study in this repo and identifies an optimal incorporation rate around 20%.

## Key Findings
- **20% biochar (v/v) was the optimum**: it significantly increased root length, volume, surface area, and average diameter across all four rootstocks.
- Root length rose **25–39%** (Sour Orange +39%, Swingle +36%, US-942 +29%, US-802 +25%); root surface area up to **+45%** (Sour Orange).
- Soil organic matter rose **118–478%** above control; soil moisture, cation exchange capacity, and **pH all increased** with biochar rate.
- Tissue/soil nutrients climbed with rate: above-ground K +16–53%; soil Mg +37–192%, Ca +42–232% (all at 30%).
- **Caveat at 30%**: root *tip* counts dropped for most rootstocks and pH rose further — more is not better. The rising-pH effect matters for citrus, which already risks Fe/Mn/Zn lockout at high pH.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly applicable as an amendment guideline: blending ~10–20% biochar into the Lisbon's bark/pumice mix can raise water and nutrient retention and CEC in a fast-draining shallow pot — a real benefit given how quickly a 14" pot leaches. Watch the pH lift: in coastal LA's already-alkaline tap water, cap biochar at ~10–15% and monitor for iron chlorosis. Pair with the pH-management and micronutrient studies.
