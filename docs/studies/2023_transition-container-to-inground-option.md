## Summary
Covers the option of eventually moving a container Lisbon into the ground, and why it may or may not fit this situation. Included for completeness of the lifecycle, since the balcony context constrains it.

## Key Findings
- A long-container-grown citrus **can be planted in-ground**, ideally in **spring after frost risk**, with care to **tease out circling roots** so they don't continue girdling.
- In-ground trees escape the container's heat, salt, and water-swing stresses — but a tree on **dwarfing rootstock stays small even in ground**, and a vigorous standard-rootstock Lisbon will become large.
- Citrus prefers **full sun, well-drained soil, pH 6–6.5** in ground; the same nutrition/pest rules apply but with far more buffering.
- The LA **ACP/HLB quarantine restricts moving citrus**, so any relocation must stay within the area and follow CDFA rules (Topic 8).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Largely not applicable to a balcony, but worth recording. A west-facing balcony has no planting bed, so the container is effectively permanent — making the root-prune/refresh holding strategy (this topic) the real long-term plan. In-ground transition only becomes relevant if the grower later gains garden space within the quarantine area; if so, do it in spring, decircle the roots, and expect a dwarfing-rootstock tree to stay compact even then.
