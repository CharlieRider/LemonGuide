## Summary
A quantitative study of how easily dried substrate components re-absorb water, and how much wetting agents help. It pins down the practical problem behind hydrophobic potting mix — the dry-out-then-won't-rewet failure that produces dry pockets and water channeling down the side of a pot.

## Key Findings
- **At 50% initial moisture**, every component except wetting-agent-free peatmoss rehydrated fully in **one irrigation event**.
- **At 25% initial moisture (severely dried), peatmoss with no wetting agent failed to reach container capacity even after 10 irrigations.** Medium/high wetting-agent rates (232–348 mL·m⁻³) restored full hydration in ~3 events.
- **Coconut coir** behaved similarly when bone-dry: no/low wetting agent never refilled; medium/high rates reached capacity in ~3 events.
- **Pine bark** also needed wetting agents at 25% moisture to rehydrate.
- **Perlite was unaffected** — rewet fully in one event regardless of moisture or wetting agent. **Shredded pine wood** rewet best of the organics (reached capacity in 3 events dry, with no wetting agent).

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Highly relevant to a west-facing LA balcony where a 14" pot can dry hard between waterings in summer. A peat- or coir-heavy mix that fully dries may shed water rather than absorb it, starving roots despite "watering." Mitigations: keep a coarse, bark/pumice/perlite-rich mix (perlite and bark fractions rewet far better), never let it bake bone-dry, and add a wetting agent if using peat/coir. Connects to the watering and overwatering studies.
