## Summary
UC IPM guidance on two microscopic **eriophyid** mites distinct from the spider mites already in this repo: the **citrus bud mite** and the **citrus rust mite (silver mite on lemons)**. Notably, both are **specifically lemon- and coast-associated** — directly on point for a Lisbon in Brentwood.

## Key Findings
- Eriophyid mites are **translucent, cigar-shaped, microscopic** — invisible without a **≥20× hand lens**; you diagnose them by their damage.
- **Citrus rust mite = "silver mite" on lemons**: feeds on exposed rind of fruit **≥0.5 inch (1.3 cm)**, destroying rind cells so lemon surfaces turn **silvery** (rust-brown on oranges); most damage **late spring–late summer**. Cosmetic.
- **Citrus bud mite**: causes **deformed buds, blossoms, foliage, and fruit**; it is an **occasional pest of lemons any time of year near the coast** (and much rarer inland or on non-lemon citrus) — i.e., a classic coastal-lemon problem.
- Both are usually held below damaging levels by predatory mites and weather; treatment (oils/sulfur/miticide) is rarely needed for home fruit.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Specifically relevant: the **citrus bud mite is a recognized coastal-lemon pest**, and the Lisbon is a lemon on a coastal LA balcony — so distorted new growth/buds may be bud mite rather than disease or nutrient problems. Damage from both mites is largely **cosmetic** (silvered rind, some bud distortion) and rarely warrants spraying on a home tree; **horticultural oil or wettable sulfur** can be used if distortion is severe (mind heat/oil cautions — see IPM-treatment study). Don't confuse with twospotted spider mites (different management). Pairs with the spider-mite, thrips, and IPM-treatment studies.
