## Summary
Explains why even a well-built bark mix doesn't last forever: organic components decompose into fines that raise water-holding and collapse air-filled porosity over time. The physical reason media must be refreshed on a schedule, not just topped up.

## Key Findings
- **Organic substrate components decompose over time**, breaking large particles into **fines that hold excess water and reduce air-filled porosity** — drifting the mix toward the hypoxia zone (Topic 1).
- High-cellulose barks (alder, poplar, maple, oak) decay fast and **immobilize nitrogen** as they break down; **pine/fir bark is far more stable** (months-to-years).
- Degradation **shrinks substrate volume** (the "sinking" seen in old pots) and worsens drainage precisely as the tree ages.
- Substrate physical properties (porosity, water/air ratio) should be **monitored, not assumed constant** over a multi-year crop.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
The mechanistic case for periodic media refresh. A 14"-deep pot that started with good 15–20% air-filled porosity (Topic 2) will lose it as bark breaks into fines — turning a once-draining mix into a soggy, hypoxia-prone one over 3–5 years. **Use durable pine/fir bark and pumice** (slow to degrade) and **fully or partially replace the mix at each root-prune** to restore aeration. This is why "just add fertilizer" can't sustain a 10-year container.
