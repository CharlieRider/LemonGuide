## Summary
How to identify the Asian citrus psyllid (ACP) and HLB symptoms, and what a homeowner should do. The actionable companion to the quarantine rules — what to look for and when to act on the balcony tree.

## Key Findings
- **ACP adults feed at a ~45° angle** on tender new flush; nymphs produce distinctive white waxy tubules; eggs are laid on new growth tips.
- **HLB leaf symptom: asymmetric blotchy mottle** (green/yellow not mirrored across the midrib — distinguishes it from nutrient deficiency, which is usually symmetric).
- **HLB fruit: small, lopsided, with aborted seeds and a bitter taste**; tree declines and dies over a few years.
- **Inspect new flush regularly**; treat ACP to protect the tree (homeowner-appropriate options include systemic/foliar insecticides and horticultural oils, timed to flush).
- **Encourage Tamarixia radiata** (parasitoid biocontrol released in CA) and avoid disrupting natural enemies where possible.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Directly actionable. Because Lisbon flushes frequently in coastal LA, there is tender growth (ACP's target) much of the year — so **flush-time ACP scouting is a year-round habit**, not seasonal. The symmetric-vs-asymmetric leaf-mottle distinction is the key field test to avoid confusing the common container micronutrient chlorosis (Topic 3) with HLB.
