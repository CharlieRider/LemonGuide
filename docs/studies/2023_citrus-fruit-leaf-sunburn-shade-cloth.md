## Summary
Covers sunburn/sunscald of citrus **leaves and fruit** (distinct from the bark-sunburn entry in the pruning topic) and the practical fix — shade cloth on the hot side. Directly addresses the west-facing-balcony risk during LA heat waves.

## Key Findings
- The **west- and south-facing sides** of a citrus tree burn first in a heat wave; leaf sunburn shows as **yellowing/browning** on sun-exposed foliage, and **fruit develops irregular brown, sunken/raised lesions** on the sun side.
- **Shade when air temperature exceeds ~95–100°F**, especially in prolonged heat waves; **50% shade cloth from ~mid-May through September** is a standard protection level for hot-climate citrus.
- **Place shade cloth on the west side** to block hot afternoon sun (the most damaging exposure); burlap is an alternative that still lets some light through.
- **Morning sun + afternoon shade** is the ideal exposure pattern — it hardens the tree without burning.
- Sunburned fruit/leaf tissue is permanently damaged and can become an entry for pests/decay.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
High priority for this exact site — a **west-facing Brentwood balcony** is the worst-case exposure for afternoon sunburn. Practical protocol for the Lisbon: hang **~50% shade cloth on the west side during summer / heat waves (>95°F)**, or position the pot to catch morning sun and afternoon shade. Protects both foliage and developing lemons. Combine with the kaolin film, heat-wave-protocol, evaporative-cooling, and bark-sunburn (pruning) studies. Note the tree itself shades its own bark/fruit if not over-pruned.
