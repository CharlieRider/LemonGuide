## Summary
UC IPM guidance on the brown garden snail (*Cornu aspersum*), an extremely common Southern California pest that climbs citrus to feed on bark, leaves, and fruit. Highly relevant to a coastal LA setting where snails are abundant and irrigation keeps surfaces moist.

## Key Findings
- Snails climb trunks to **chew leaves, young bark (ring-barking twigs), and rind**, and shelter in the canopy by day.
- **Copper barrier on the trunk is the key exclusion tactic**: a **copper foil band 1–2 ft above ground with ~8-inch overlap** (allows trunk growth) repels snails for years; alternatives are a Bordeaux/tribasic-copper-sulfate slurry band.
- **Skirt-pruning** (lifting low branches) removes the bridges snails use to reach the canopy from the ground — combine with the copper band.
- **Hand-removal** (especially at night/after irrigation) and iron-phosphate baits reduce numbers; the predatory **decollate snail** is a long-term biocontrol (but is killed by baits).
- Remove ground debris/hiding spots near the tree.

## Relevance to 19" × 14" Container
Very practical for a balcony Lisbon: a small potted tree is easy to protect by **banding the trunk (or pot rim) with copper tape** and keeping the canopy skirt clear of the pot surface and nearby surfaces, denying snails a climbing route. Hand-pick after evening watering. On an elevated balcony, isolating the pot (copper rim + no touching foliage bridges) can exclude snails almost entirely. Connects to the skirt-pruning, ant-management, and IPM-treatment studies.
